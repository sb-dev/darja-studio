# Algerian Darja conversational course specification

## Product

Create a complete beginner-to-independent-speaker course provisionally titled:

**Speak Algerian Arabic: Quick, Natural and Rooted**

The course takes the strongest principle of *Conversational Arabic Quick and
Easy: Palestinian Dialect*: teach a compact set of high-frequency,
structurally connected words that learners can combine immediately instead of
memorising disconnected lists.

Improve that method for Algerian Darja through:

- natural dialogues and connected speech;
- earlier use of past and future;
- Arabic script, consistent pedagogical transliteration and audio together;
- explicit conversational repair;
- productive versus recognition vocabulary;
- bounded regional variation;
- realistic Arabic–French code-switching;
- cultural learning through situations;
- food and music as language material;
- careful links to coping, adaptation, memory and resistance during and after
  French colonisation.

The course should be usable for self-study and suitable for later production
as a book, web course and audio course.

## Learner

An English-speaking adult beginner who wants to converse naturally with
Algerians, follow family and neighbourhood conversation, understand common
regional differences and engage respectfully with Algerian culture and
history.

Do not assume knowledge of Arabic script. Script learning must support
speaking rather than delay it.

## Dialect policy

Use a broadly useful central urban Algerian Darja teaching baseline, associated
mainly with Algiers, but never present it as the only Algerian form.

Add recognition notes for western, eastern, Saharan and Amazigh-influenced
speech when differences are important. State the limits of evidence. Prefer
phrasing such as “you may hear” over “everyone in this region says”.

Distinguish:

- ordinary Darja;
- Modern Standard Arabic;
- integrated French-derived vocabulary;
- active French code-switching;
- Amazigh-origin or Amazigh-influenced language;
- regional and generational forms.

All productive expressions require credible evidence and should be flagged
where native-speaker validation is still needed.

## Vocabulary design

Target approximately:

- 380 productive items;
- 150 recognition items.

Prioritise:

1. conversational utility;
2. combinability with earlier words;
3. recurrence;
4. pronunciation value;
5. cultural value;
6. manageable learning cost.

A chapter should normally add 10–16 productive items. Culturally necessary but
low-frequency words belong in recognition vocabulary.

Recycle new productive language in:

- the same chapter;
- another chapter in the section;
- a review or listening task;
- the section exit task.

## Standard chapter structure

Each chapter should contain:

1. communicative outcome;
2. realistic situation;
3. productive vocabulary;
4. recognition vocabulary;
5. reusable sentence frames;
6. dialogue A in accessible natural speech;
7. dialogue B in ordinary speech;
8. Arabic script, transliteration and natural English;
9. one pronunciation or rhythm focus;
10. pragmatic and social guidance;
11. bounded regional recognition;
12. cultural or historical material when relevant;
13. substitution practice;
14. retrieval practice;
15. listening task;
16. information-gap or personalised speaking;
17. TTS-ready scripts and recording notes;
18. source list and native-review questions.

Do not create dialogues whose only purpose is displaying vocabulary.

## Research requirements

Use credible research for linguistic, regional, cultural and historical claims.

Prefer:

1. corpora and documented recordings;
2. peer-reviewed research;
3. Algerian universities and Algerian scholars;
4. institutional cultural sources;
5. specialist dictionaries and teaching material;
6. native-speaker elicitation;
7. informal usage only as evidence that a form occurs.

Search in English, French and Arabic. Include direct searches for the target
expression in Arabic script, likely spelling variants and Arabizi when useful.

For colonial or politically sensitive historical material, seek Algerian
sources and independent scholarly or primary evidence. Separate:

- documented event;
- personal memory;
- collective narrative;
- editorial interpretation.

Never invent proverbs, etymologies, quotations, song lyrics or historical
causal claims. Do not reproduce copyrighted lyrics.

## Colonial-history and resilience policy

Do not force colonial history into every domestic topic.

Use it when a documented mechanism connects the language lesson to:

- language hierarchy and education;
- oral transmission;
- preservation of names and places;
- family and neighbourhood solidarity;
- food continuity and adaptation;
- music and coded expression;
- humour and indirect speech;
- resistance and logistical support;
- migration and remittance networks;
- women’s practical and mnemonic work;
- multilingual appropriation.

Represent Algeria through ordinary life, pleasure, creativity and contemporary
ambition as well as suffering and resistance.

## Audio and TTS policy

This text-generation project produces scripts and manifests, not final audio.

Each important utterance should have plans for:

1. careful phrase;
2. natural phrase;
3. shadowing phrase groups;
4. response or contrast in dialogue.

TTS can prototype vocabulary drills, substitution drills and slow review.
Native recording is preferred for:

- authoritative anchor dialogues;
- regional comparisons;
- code-switching;
- humour and teasing;
- hospitality and politeness;
- music-influenced speech;
- emotionally sensitive history and testimony.

For every audio item specify:

- clip ID;
- text in Arabic script;
- transliteration;
- meaning;
- speaker region and profile;
- relationship and setting;
- pace;
- emotional intent;
- pauses;
- code-switching;
- pronunciation risks;
- whether TTS is acceptable;
- whether human recording is required.

Do not ask a generic TTS model to fabricate a regional accent without suitable
training or licensed reference audio.

# Course outline

## Part I — Building the Algerian sentence engine

### Section 1 — Hearing and entering the language

#### Chapter 1 — What Algerian Arabic sounds like

Algerian rhythm, reduced vowels, joined words, essential consonants, careful
versus rapid speech, Arabic script, transliteration, Arabizi and regional
pronunciation preview.

Cultural strand: Darja as an oral, adaptive language rather than deficient
Standard Arabic.

#### Chapter 2 — Greeting people naturally

Greetings, wellbeing, welcome, goodbye, formality, warmth, repetition,
addressing individuals and groups, older people and entering a home.

#### Chapter 3 — Who I am and where I come from

Pronouns, name, origin, residence, regional identities, nationalities and
languages spoken or understood.

Historical strand: names, place names and identity under colonial
administration.

#### Chapter 4 — Understanding and repairing conversation

Understanding, non-understanding, repetition, slower speech, asking meaning,
asking how something is said and correcting misunderstanding politely.

Resilience strand: translation and code-switching as practical intelligence.

## Section 2 — The words that generate conversation

#### Chapter 5 — Having, wanting and needing

Have, want, need, like, can, cannot, enough, still and already.

#### Chapter 6 — This, that, here and there

Demonstratives, location, who, what, which, where, existence, something and
nothing.

#### Chapter 7 — Asking useful questions

Who, what, where, why, how, when, how much, really and then. Include intonation.

#### Chapter 8 — Negation without grammar tables

`ma…sh`, common negative phrases, never, not yet, no longer and softened
refusal.

#### Chapter 9 — Joining thoughts

And, but, because, so, also, maybe, if, then, before, after, normally and
examples.

Section exit: introduce oneself, ask questions, explain a simple problem and
repair misunderstanding.

## Part II — Everyday Algerian life

### Section 3 — Home, family and neighbourhood

#### Chapter 10 — Family and belonging

Family, possession, age relationships, marriage and respectful enquiry.

Resilience strand: extended family networks as care and continuity during
displacement, conflict and migration.

#### Chapter 11 — At home

Rooms, household objects, enter, sit, wait, bring, open, close, put, take,
clean, ready, hot, cold and location.

#### Chapter 12 — The neighbourhood

Neighbour, street, building, shop, proximity, safety, crowds, local knowledge
and asking for help.

Cultural strand: the *houma* as place and social identity.

#### Chapter 13 — Visiting someone

Entering, sitting, hospitality, coffee or tea, polite insistence, refusal,
staying and departure.

Avoid simplistic rules such as fixed numbers of refusals.

### Section 4 — Eating, shopping and moving around

#### Chapter 14 — Tea, coffee and the café

Ordering drinks, sugar, milk, another serving, the bill, waiting and meeting.

Cultural strand: café life, news, debate, work, music and observation.

#### Chapter 15 — Eating together

Hunger, thirst, fullness, eating, drinking, tasting, serving, flavour, more,
enough and table expressions.

#### Chapter 16 — The Algerian kitchen

Teach food through action verbs: cut, wash, mix, boil, fry, bake, add, leave
and bring.

Recognition references may include chorba frik, kesra, rechta, couscous,
mhajeb, karantika, makroud and qalb el louz.

Cultural strand: recipes as oral family archives.

Resilience strand: adaptation during scarcity, migration and exile.

#### Chapter 17 — At the market and shops

Prices, expense, quantity, weight, quality, freshness, colours, sizes and
socially appropriate negotiation.

#### Chapter 18 — Numbers, money and time

Numbers in real exchanges, prices, telephone numbers, time, days, dayparts,
early and late.

#### Chapter 19 — Finding your way

Movement, directions, transport, duration, being lost, destinations and
landmark-based navigation.

## Part III — Expanding beyond the present

### Section 5 — What happened and what will happen

#### Chapter 20 — Talking about yesterday

High-frequency past forms, yesterday, last week, what happened and event
questions.

#### Chapter 21 — Telling a short story

Sequence markers, suddenly, fortunately, unfortunately, reported speech,
thought and description.

Cultural strand: repetition, exaggeration and performance in storytelling.

#### Chapter 22 — Plans and intentions

Near future, plans, later, soon, next week, perhaps, God willing and
conditional ability.

#### Chapter 23 — Invitations and arrangements

Availability, meeting time, being busy, alternatives, being on the way and
being nearly there.

#### Chapter 24 — Work, study and obligations

Work, study, start, finish, jobs, school, university, tiredness, busyness,
lateness, completion and skills.

Historical strand: colonial education and language hierarchy, followed by
post-independence multilingual realities.

## Part IV — Relationships, feelings and social intelligence

### Section 6 — Speaking like a person, not a phrasebook

#### Chapter 25 — Likes, dislikes and opinions

Preference, thought, opinion, agreement, disagreement and evaluative
adjectives.

#### Chapter 26 — Feelings and wellbeing

Happiness, sadness, worry, anger, fear, comfort, missing someone, frustration,
reassurance and easing hardship.

#### Chapter 27 — Affection, friendship and trust

Friendship, love, trust, remembering, missing someone, care, reassurance and
solidarity.

#### Chapter 28 — Disagreement and boundaries

Refusal, disagreement, enough, privacy, calming down and speaking properly.
Compare direct and indirect confrontation.

#### Chapter 29 — Humour, teasing and indirect speech

Joking, disbelief, playful address, understatement, exaggeration and tone.

Resilience strand: humour, ambiguity and coded speech under hardship and
authority.

#### Chapter 30 — Proverbs and compressed wisdom

Teach only verified, genuinely useful proverbs through situations. Focus on
patience, neighbours, family, work, fortune, hospitality, appearances and
self-reliance. Teach how to respond, not merely translate.

## Part V — Algeria through food, music and place

### Section 7 — Cultural fluency

#### Chapter 31 — Travelling across Algeria

Coast, mountains, desert, village, city, selected places, origins, regional
identity and asking for local wording.

#### Chapter 32 — The many voices of Algeria

Darja, Standard Arabic, Tamazight varieties, French, western Spanish influence,
Ottoman and Mediterranean vocabulary, social meaning and code-switching.

Teach recognition and strategies rather than stereotypes.

#### Chapter 33 — Chaâbi and the city

Listening vocabulary, love, absence, patience, fortune, urban setting, the
Casbah, cafés and poetry becoming conversational language.

Do not reproduce lyrics.

#### Chapter 34 — Raï: saying what cannot easily be said

Love, desire, departure, social pressure, western pronunciation,
Arabic–French alternation and informed references to major performers.

Use original practice lines rather than lyrics.

#### Chapter 35 — Andalusi, Malouf, Kabyle and Diwan traditions

Introduce major traditions, place, occasion, instruments, performers and
audiences without flattening them into one genre.

#### Chapter 36 — Celebrations and collective life

Weddings, Ramadan evenings, Eid, births, visits, clothing, food,
congratulations, blessings and condolences.

## Part VI — Language, colonisation and ways of enduring

### Section 8 — Memory and resilience

This section teaches language through personal stories, family conversation,
songs, places and objects rather than becoming a detached history textbook.

#### Chapter 37 — Before, during and after

Time framing, long ago, arrival, taking, changing, remaining, keeping,
independence and freedom.

#### Chapter 38 — Land, home and displacement

Land, home, village, city, ownership, loss, departure, return, documents and
ancestral places.

Coping strand: names, stories, recipes, graves, visits and family memory.

#### Chapter 39 — School, language and imposed hierarchy

School, teachers, books, permission, prohibition, reading, writing and
learning. Explain French, Arabic and Amazigh language hierarchy with evidence.

#### Chapter 40 — Resistance, secrecy and solidarity

Resist, hide, help, protect, carry, secret, message, news, trust and betrayal.
Distinguish survival, accommodation and collaboration.

#### Chapter 41 — Women, oral memory and invisible work

Women’s combatant and non-combatant roles, domestic, medical, logistical and
oral-memory work. Examine whose experiences enter official history.

#### Chapter 42 — Migration, exile and the journey to France

Leaving, arriving, work, papers, borders, loneliness, remittances, return,
diaspora children and intergenerational language.

#### Chapter 43 — Memory without rehearsed slogans

Remembering, uncertainty, family accounts, disagreement, evidence, pain,
pride, silence and inheritance.

Do not reduce Algerians to victims, romanticise suffering or present one
family memory as the national story.

## Part VII — Contemporary Algerian conversation

### Section 9 — Speaking in the present-day world

#### Chapter 44 — Phones, messages and Arabizi

Calling, sending, answering, recording, voice notes, online status, common
digit substitutions, spelling variation and movement between scripts.

Keep pedagogical transliteration separate from informal Arabizi.

#### Chapter 45 — News, rumours and credibility

Hearing claims, truth, uncertainty, sources, verification and distinguishing
information from gossip.

#### Chapter 46 — Contemporary aspirations and frustrations

Housing, work, prices, travel, education, family expectations, opportunity,
hope and uncertainty. Discuss *harga* carefully and without romanticisation.

#### Chapter 47 — Hosting a complete conversation

Integrate greeting, introduction, family, food, past events, plans, opinions,
emotion, clarification and departure.

#### Chapter 48 — Listening to Algeria

Authentic-style voices from different regions, generations and social settings.
Teach identification of sentence structure, tone and key information without
requiring perfect comprehension.

#### Chapter 49 — Final project: one family, one neighbourhood, one story

The learner produces a spoken dossier containing self-introduction, family or
community, food, music, memory, history, present concern and future hope.

# Appendices

Generate concise usable appendices:

- productive core vocabulary organised by function;
- high-frequency verb patterns;
- regional listening guide;
- French borrowing and code-switching guide;
- food and music map;
- concise historical timeline;
- fifty-expression conversation survival sheet;
- audio programme and recording manifest guidance.

# Completion criteria

The assembled course must:

- contain all nine sections and forty-nine chapters;
- progress cumulatively;
- avoid uncontrolled vocabulary growth;
- provide real conversational outcomes;
- bound regional claims;
- cite factual cultural and historical claims;
- flag native-review needs;
- contain TTS-ready scripts without pretending TTS replaces native speakers;
- connect language, culture and history without turning every chapter into a
  historical essay;
- remain readable and practical rather than becoming a linguistic monograph.
