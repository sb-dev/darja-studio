# Course production standard

**Course:** *Speak Algerian Arabic: Quick, Natural and Rooted*  
**Artefact:** Course foundation: method, dialect, notation, evidence and audio  
**Status:** Binding production standard  
**Version:** 1.0 — 17 July 2026  
**Applies to:** Introduction, all nine sections, all forty-nine chapters, reviews, appendices, lexical ledger and audio manifests

---

## 1. Purpose and non-negotiable principles

This document defines how every part of the course must be researched, written, reviewed and recorded. It does **not** prescribe a national standard for Algerian Darja and must never be presented as doing so. Published work describes Algerian Arabic as internally variable and not governed by one universally accepted everyday orthography; dedicated resources have therefore proposed conventional spellings for particular research purposes rather than documenting a single settled public standard. ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208/); [Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Publications/ViewPaper?Code=ijacsa&Issue=3&SerialNo=53&Volume=7)). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208/?utm_source=openai))

The course must:

1. Teach **spoken Algerian Darja for interaction**, not Modern Standard Arabic with colloquial vocabulary substituted into it.
2. Use a consistent **central urban baseline associated mainly with Algiers and its surroundings**, chosen for course coherence rather than declared national superiority.
3. Treat western, eastern, Saharan and Amazigh-influenced speech as real parts of Algeria’s linguistic landscape, introduced through bounded recognition notes rather than mixed indiscriminately into productive lessons.
4. teach high-utility sentence frames, repair strategies and connected speech before large thematic word lists.
5. introduce the past and future early enough for learners to discuss actual experience and plans.
6. distinguish ordinary Darja, MSA, integrated loans, active French code-switching, Amazigh contact, and regional or generational forms.
7. give Arabic script, pedagogical transliteration and natural English for important utterances.
8. use retrieval, spaced recurrence and purposeful tasks rather than repetition alone. Research supports retrieval with feedback for spoken L2 vocabulary and supports interaction through well-designed information-gap tasks. ([Kang, Gollan & Pashler 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23681928/); [Ellis et al. 2021](https://www.cambridge.org/core/elements/taskbased-language-teaching/395B3D3B0F7078DF325579CC8314E38B)). ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23681928/?utm_source=openai))
9. portray ordinary life, humour, work, family, food, creativity and present ambitions alongside difficult history.
10. produce scripts and manifests for audio, not fabricated regional audio.

## 2. The compact sentence engine

### 2.1 Definition

A **sentence engine** is a small, reusable network of:

- a communicative intention;
- a stable frame or interactional sequence;
- replaceable slots;
- the minimum grammar needed to operate it;
- repair language for when it fails;
- sound and rhythm practice;
- repeated retrieval in new situations.

A chapter is not organised as “twenty words about transport.” It is organised around something a person needs to accomplish, such as:

> identify a destination → check understanding → state a preference → correct a misunderstanding → confirm the result

A frame is taught as meaningful language first, then opened into replaceable parts. Formulaic sequences can support spoken fluency, but the course must also require learners to retrieve and adapt them rather than merely imitate them. ([McGuire & Larson-Hall 2018](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1174049.pdf); [Kang, Gollan & Pashler 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23681928/)). ([files.eric.ed.gov](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1174049.pdf?utm_source=openai))

### 2.2 Required teaching cycle

Every chapter follows this cycle:

1. **Need:** establish a believable reason to speak.
2. **Notice:** hear or read the target language in a short purposeful exchange.
3. **Understand:** identify speaker goal, relationship, setting and outcome.
4. **Build:** isolate one to three reusable frames and their slots.
5. **Sound:** practise one high-value pronunciation or rhythm feature.
6. **Substitute:** change controlled slots without changing the communicative purpose.
7. **Retrieve:** produce from an English meaning, image, prompt or interlocutor cue.
8. **Interact:** complete an information-gap, decision or personalised task.
9. **Repair:** repeat the task while requiring at least one clarification or correction.
10. **Recycle:** recall earlier language inside the new task.

### 2.3 Frame specification

Each frame must be stored in the chapter and lexical ledger with:

- frame ID;
- communicative function;
- Arabic-script skeleton;
- transliterated skeleton;
- natural English range;
- fixed material;
- replaceable slots and slot constraints;
- agreement or conjugation behaviour;
- negation pattern;
- pragmatic restrictions;
- minimum two natural examples;
- linked earlier and later frames;
- evidence and validation status.

### 2.4 What does not qualify as a sentence engine

The following are insufficient:

- an isolated conjugation table with no situation;
- a dialogue whose only purpose is to display every new word;
- substitution where semantically impossible words fit the same printed blank;
- memorising Arabic script before speaking;
- an English sentence translated word for word into Darja;
- a cultural paragraph unrelated to the chapter’s language or task.

## 3. Dialect baseline and regional policy

### 3.1 Productive baseline

Productive language will use a **broadly useful central urban variety associated principally with contemporary Algiers and its periphery**. Research resources have treated Algiers as a distinct city variety and have documented it separately in corpora and dialect descriptions. ([Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf); [MADAR Corpus](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1535/)). ([thesai.org](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf?utm_source=openai))

“Central urban” is an editorial baseline, not a claim that:

- all residents of Algiers speak identically;
- Algiers speech is socially neutral in every setting;
- the baseline is understood or preferred equally everywhere;
- other Algerian varieties are deviations from a correct form.

Variation can occur by neighbourhood, family history, mobility, age, education, multilingual repertoire, relationship and topic. Writers must not collapse all variation into geography.

### 3.2 Productive consistency rule

For any productive grammatical variable, the course selects **one reviewed baseline form** and uses it consistently until a later chapter explicitly teaches a choice. A chapter may not alternate forms merely because both were found online.

An alternative becomes productive only if it is:

1. broadly useful;
2. communicatively necessary;
3. supported by suitable evidence;
4. reviewed for the represented community;
5. added to the lexical ledger with a clear relationship to the baseline form.

### 3.3 Bounded recognition notes

Regional material must appear in a box headed **“You may hear…”** and contain:

- region or community stated as narrowly as evidence permits;
- the baseline form first;
- the alternative form;
- whether the difference concerns pronunciation, vocabulary, grammar or pragmatics;
- whether learners should recognise it only or may safely use it locally;
- source and native-review status;
- no more than three contrasts in an ordinary chapter.

Use formulations such as:

- “In parts of western Algeria, you may hear…”
- “Some speakers in and around…”
- “Among some younger bilingual speakers…”
- “This recording documents X; it does not establish use throughout the region.”

Do not write “Algerians say…” when evidence is local.

The reflex of historical **qāf**, for example, can vary as *q, g, ʔ* or *k* across Algerian locations and lexical items; it must therefore be entered and reviewed item by item rather than converted by a national rule. ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf); [Souag 2005](https://soas-repository.worktribe.com/output/417604/notes-on-the-algerian-arabic-dialect-of-dellys)). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf?utm_source=openai))

### 3.4 Regional comparison limits

- Regional forms are normally **recognition vocabulary**.
- Never direct generic TTS to create an Oran, Constantine, Kabyle, Saharan or other regional accent.
- A regional comparison clip requires a speaker with an appropriate lived linguistic profile and review by another competent speaker from the represented area.
- Regional accents must not be used as comic effects.
- Labels such as “east,” “west” and “south” are planning categories, not internally uniform dialects.

## 4. Language-layer taxonomy

Every lexical item, example and audio line receives one or more language-layer tags.

| Tag | Layer | Editorial treatment |
|---|---|---|
| `DA-CORE` | Ordinary baseline Darja | Default productive layer. |
| `DA-REG` | Regional/local Darja | Normally recognition; specify location and evidence. |
| `MSA` | Modern Standard Arabic or formal Arabic accommodation | Label explicitly; do not present as ordinary street conversation. |
| `FR-INT` | French-derived item integrated into Darja | Darja phonology/morphology or established everyday use; treated as a Darja lexical item. |
| `FR-CS` | Active French code-switch | Preserve the switch as French; include speaker, setting and pragmatic rationale. |
| `AMZ-CONTACT` | Documented Amazigh-origin or Amazigh-influenced item/pattern | Use only with linguistic evidence; name the relevant variety where known. |
| `OTHER-LOAN` | Integrated loan from another documented source | Name source only when evidence is adequate. |
| `GEN` | Generationally associated usage | State the observed group and date/source; never equate “young” with all young Algerians. |
| `SOC` | Social/professional/register-associated usage | Define the setting and speakers represented. |
| `UNCERTAIN` | Layer or origin unresolved | Never state a firm etymology; normally withhold from productive inventory. |

### 4.1 Darja versus MSA

Darja and MSA differ across phonology, morphology, vocabulary and syntax; the course must not treat Darja merely as carelessly pronounced MSA. ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208/); [Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf)). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf?utm_source=openai))

MSA may be included for:

- recognising signs, announcements or formal terms;
- strategic accommodation with another Arabic speaker;
- explaining script relationships;
- contrasting an artificial textbook form with the ordinary Darja form.

MSA does not enter the productive count unless the learner is explicitly expected to use it as an accommodation strategy.

### 4.2 Integrated French-derived vocabulary

An item is tagged `FR-INT` when it behaves as part of Darja in the documented use under consideration—for example through phonological adaptation, Darja articles, possession, pluralisation or verb morphology. Work on Algiers speech distinguishes phonologically adapted French-origin items from stretches retaining French phonology. ([Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf), pp. 389–391). ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/299559852_An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources))

An integrated loan is **not** printed as an optional French decoration. It is taught as part of the actual Darja sentence engine, with its Darja pronunciation and grammar.

### 4.3 Active French code-switching

An item or stretch is tagged `FR-CS` when the speaker switches into recognisably French material rather than using a fully integrated Darja item. Algerian Arabic–French code-switching is documented in both spontaneous speech corpora and online corpora, but its density and functions vary with speakers and situations. ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf); [CAFE dataset](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12648582/)). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/?utm_source=openai))

Rules:

- do not imply that French is required for authentic Algerian identity;
- provide a credible Darja alternative when one is available;
- do not assign code-switching to a speaker solely because of class, gender or education;
- preserve French spelling in the transcript while supplying pronunciation help separately;
- require human recording for meaningful code-switched dialogue;
- mark whether the switch is lexical, phrasal, quoted, technical, humorous or interactional.

### 4.4 Amazigh contact

Arabic–Amazigh contact is long-standing and bidirectional, but specific origins and structural claims require specialist evidence. The sociolinguistic conditions and outcomes vary substantially by community. ([Kossmann 2017](https://doi.org/10.1093/ACREFORE/9780199384655.013.232); [Souag 2020](https://langsci-press.org/catalog/view/235/1820/1846-1)). ([doi.org](https://doi.org/10.1093/ACREFORE%2F9780199384655.013.232?utm_source=openai))

Rules:

- “Amazigh” is not one homogeneous source language; identify Kabyle, Chaoui, Mozabite or another variety when the evidence does.
- resemblance is not proof of origin;
- native intuition may establish use, but not etymology by itself;
- uncertain proposed origins receive `AMZ?` and `UNCERTAIN`, not a definitive cultural note;
- do not use an unverified word-origin story to decorate a lesson.

### 4.5 Official-language context

Arabic and Tamazight are national and official languages under Algeria’s constitutional framework. French has extensive social and institutional use but is not constitutionally designated an official language. The course must distinguish legal status from actual language practice. ([Constitution of Algeria, arts. 3–4](https://www.wipo.int/wipolex/edocs/lexdocs/laws/ar/dz/dz018ar.pdf); [British Academy, *Multilingualism in Algeria*](https://www.thebritishacademy.ac.uk/publishing/journal-british-academy/10s4/multilingualism-in-algeria/)). ([wipo.int](https://www.wipo.int/wipolex/edocs/lexdocs/laws/ar/dz/dz018ar.pdf?utm_source=openai))

## 5. Arabic-script standard

### 5.1 Purpose

Arabic script supports literacy, dictionary use and recognition of Algerian writing. It must support speaking rather than delay it. Because everyday Darja spelling varies, the course uses a **consistent pedagogical Arabic script**, accompanied by transliteration and audio.

This is an editorial convention, not a claim to reproduce all social-media spellings or to replace CODA-style research orthographies.

### 5.2 General rules

1. Write Arabic-origin words in a stable, recognisable Arabic form, but do not restore MSA endings or grammar absent from speech.
2. Do not write case endings.
3. Normally omit short-vowel diacritics.
4. Add a short vowel, sukūn or shadda only where it resolves a beginner-relevant ambiguity.
5. Show pronounced gemination with shadda where useful; transliteration must always double the consonant.
6. Write attached Darja clitics consistently in Arabic; use pedagogical hyphens only in transliteration and analysis.
7. Use ordinary Arabic punctuation plus question marks where useful.
8. Do not force active French switches into ambiguous Arabic spelling.

### 5.3 Additional letters

| Sound | Preferred letter | Policy |
|---|---|---|
| /g/ | ڨ | Use when the reviewed lexical pronunciation is /g/. Also list common encountered spellings such as ق where relevant. |
| /p/ | پ | Use chiefly in integrated loans where /p/ is retained; otherwise reflect the reviewed pronunciation. |
| /v/ | ڤ | Use chiefly in integrated loans where /v/ is retained. |

These letters reflect sounds found in loanword and dialect writing, but real-world spelling remains variable. Algerian orthography proposals likewise extend Arabic script for sounds absent from the basic Arabic inventory. ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf); [Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf)). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf))

### 5.4 French inside Arabic-script dialogue

- **Integrated loan:** write in Arabic script and transliterate as pronounced in Darja.
- **Active switch:** retain standard French in Latin script inside the Arabic line, for example: `راني en retard.` This mixed-script policy is deliberate.
- If a publisher requires a fully Arabic-script display, add an editorial rendering in a separate field, never as the authoritative pronunciation record.

### 5.5 Search and encounter variants

Each ledger entry may record:

- `arabic_course_form` — the form printed in the course;
- `arabic_attested_variants` — searchable observed forms;
- `arabizi_attested_variants`;
- `french_spellings`;
- `msa_related_form` where pedagogically useful.

Attested variants are for recognition and search. They do not automatically become acceptable productive spellings.

## 6. Pedagogical transliteration

### 6.1 Principles

The transliteration is:

- pronunciation-oriented but not narrow IPA;
- consistent across the course;
- readable by English-speaking adults;
- distinct from informal Arabizi;
- explicit about consonant length and important vowel length;
- subordinate to reviewed audio where natural speech varies.

### 6.2 Vowels

| Symbol | Teaching value | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| `a` | short open vowel | Approximately the vowel in *cat* or *cut*, depending on surrounding consonants; follow audio. |
| `e` | short central/reduced vowel | Usually schwa-like; never assume English spelling. |
| `i` | short front vowel | Short *i*-like quality. |
| `u` | short back rounded vowel | Short *u*-like quality. |
| `o` | rounded mid vowel, especially in loans | Use only when supported by the reviewed pronunciation. |
| `aa` | long/open vowel | Hold longer than `a`. |
| `ii` | long front vowel | Approximately *ee*, held. |
| `uu` | long back rounded vowel | Approximately *oo*, held. |

The notation is pedagogical: it does not claim that every surface vowel quality has the same phonological status in every Algerian variety. Reduced vowels and short-vowel deletion are important in Maghrebi speech and must be taught through audio and rhythm, not inferred from MSA spelling. ([Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf)). ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/299559852_An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources))

### 6.3 Consonants

| Symbol | Approximation or instruction |
|---|---|
| `b t d k m n f s z l r w y h` | Broadly as expected; audio controls exact quality. |
| `sh` | English *sh*. |
| `zh` | Sound in English *measure*; common central-urban teaching value for ج where reviewed. |
| `j` | English *j*; use only for an actually affricated pronunciation. |
| `kh` | Voiceless sound like German *Bach* or Scottish *loch*. |
| `gh` | Voiced counterpart of `kh`. |
| `ḥ` | Strong breathy/pharyngeal ح; not English `h`. |
| `ʿ` | ع; a pharyngeal constriction. Beginners may initially approximate, but it remains written. |
| `q` | Uvular stop ق, farther back than `k`. |
| `g` | Hard `g` as in *go*. |
| `ṭ ḍ ṣ ẓ` | Emphatic/backed consonants; backing often affects nearby vowels. |
| `th dh` | Interdentals where genuinely pronounced; commonly recognition or regional/lexical. |
| `’` | Audible glottal stop only when needed for learning or contrast. |

### 6.4 Gemination

Double consonants are held longer and may distinguish words or grammar:

- transliteration: `mm`, `tt`, `dd`, `kk`, etc.;
- Arabic: shadda where useful;
- recording note: mark `[hold]` only in the pronunciation track, not in learner-facing text.

### 6.5 Word boundaries and hyphens

Use hyphens to make a teaching boundary visible, not to represent a pause:

- article: `l-`, with spoken assimilation shown where pedagogically useful;
- attached pronoun: `-i`, `-ek`, `-u`, etc.;
- discontinuous negation: `ma …-sh`;
- preposition plus suffix where being analysed.

Running dialogue may remove analytical hyphens after the pattern is established, but ledger headwords retain them.

### 6.6 Connected-speech notation

Learner-facing dialogue uses normal transliteration. A separate pronunciation line may use:

- `_` for a close rhythm group: `ma_fhemt-sh`;
- `‿` for linking without a pause;
- `/` for a short thought-group boundary;
- `//` for a turn-final or strong boundary;
- **bold** for the main beat;
- `(e)` for a weak vowel that may be reduced or absent in ordinary speed, only after native review.

Do not place narrow phonetic detail in the main dialogue.

### 6.7 Transliteration examples

The following illustrate notation only and do not pre-approve a complete chapter inventory:

| Arabic | Transliteration | Meaning | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| راني هنا | `raani hna` | I’m here. | Illustrative; baseline pronunciation `NV-OPEN`. |
| ما فهمتش | `ma fhemt-sh` | I didn’t understand. | Illustrative; vowel/rhythm `NV-OPEN`. |

## 7. Arabizi policy

Arabizi is informal Latin-script writing whose conventions vary by writer, platform and community. Algerian corpora document both Arabic and Latin-script writing and substantial spelling variability. ([Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf); [Touileb & Barnes 2021](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.07400)). ([cis.upenn.edu](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf?utm_source=openai))

Course policy:

1. Arabizi is **recognition literacy**, not the main pronunciation system.
2. The course transliteration never substitutes `3`, `7`, `9` or similar numerals for Arabic consonants.
3. An appendix introduces common mappings such as `3 → ع`, `7 → ح`, and locally variable uses of other digits.
4. Every Arabizi example must be attested or elicited; editors must not mechanically convert course transliteration into “authentic” Arabizi.
5. Preserve original spelling in cited messages, with privacy protection and a normalised course line beneath it.
6. Arabizi variants do not count as additional vocabulary items.

## 8. Productive and recognition vocabulary

### 8.1 Labels

- **`[P] Productive`** — learners must understand, retrieve and use the item in a communicative task.
- **`[R] Recognition`** — learners should understand it in context but are not required to produce it.
- **`[P→R] Demoted`** — initially proposed as productive but moved to recognition after evidence or load review.
- **`[R→P] Promoted`** — promotion requires validation, ledger approval and recurrence planning.

Every item also carries a language-layer tag from Section 4.

### 8.2 What counts as one lexical item

Count one ledger unit for:

- one lexical lemma or lexical family taught as a unit;
- one non-compositional multiword expression;
- one high-frequency discourse or repair chunk;
- one active code-switch taught for deliberate use.

Do not count separately:

- predictable inflected forms of the same verb;
- regular gender or number forms taught as a paradigm;
- Arabic-script and transliterated versions;
- a pronunciation variant;
- each slot-filled example of the same frame.

A suppletive, semantically unpredictable or separately memorised form may receive its own ledger item with an explicit rationale.

### 8.3 Resolving the numerical brief

The course target is approximately **380 unique productive lexical units** and **150 unique recognition units**. Forty-nine chapters cannot each add ten unique productive lexical units without exceeding that target. Therefore:

- a typical chapter has an **active target set of 10–16 items**;
- normally **6–10 are newly counted productive lexical units**;
- the remainder are new grammar operations, transparent forms, or deliberately recycled productive items;
- chapter metadata must show both `new_unique_P` and `active_target_set`.

This interpretation keeps the course within the lexical budget while preserving the requested chapter workload.

### 8.4 Section allocation

| Section | Chapters | New productive units | New recognition units | Main progression |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 1 | 5 | 36 | 12 | First contact, identity, needs, repair and basic states |
| 2 | 5 | 38 | 14 | Daily actions, places, requests and present-time interaction |
| 3 | 6 | 45 | 17 | Past events, movement, food and short narratives |
| 4 | 6 | 48 | 19 | Plans, future reference, invitations and arrangements |
| 5 | 6 | 48 | 19 | Home, family, shopping, quantities and comparison |
| 6 | 6 | 46 | 19 | Work, study, services, reasons and problem-solving |
| 7 | 5 | 42 | 18 | Experiences, health, travel and more complex transactions |
| 8 | 5 | 40 | 17 | Stories, opinions, media, humour and reported information |
| 9 | 5 | 37 | 15 | Independent conversation, nuance, variation and repair under pressure |
| **Total** | **49** | **380** | **150** | |

Section editors may move up to three items between adjacent sections, but the master ledger must retain the total and record the reason.

### 8.5 Category guardrails

- Active productive French switches should normally remain **below 20 of the 380 units**.
- Every productive `FR-CS` item should have a Darja alternative or an explicit explanation of why none is being taught.
- Do not impose an etymological quota on integrated loans.
- Do not create an “Amazigh word quota”; include relevant, well-supported material naturally.
- Names, transparent numbers generated by a taught system, and one-off culture terms do not automatically enter the productive count.

## 9. Recurrence and retrieval rules

### 9.1 Productive-item recurrence

A new productive item must receive:

1. **Introduction:** at least one meaningful occurrence in input.
2. **Immediate noticing:** learner identifies meaning or function.
3. **Controlled production:** at least two successful substitutions or responses.
4. **Same-chapter retrieval:** production without copying.
5. **Near recurrence:** meaningful reuse within the next two chapters.
6. **Section recurrence:** reuse later in the same section outside its original topic.
7. **Exit recurrence:** availability in the section exit task or its preparation.
8. **Long recurrence:** at least one later return in another section for high-utility items.

Core repair expressions, pronouns, high-frequency verbs and discourse organisers require recurrence in at least **five chapters after introduction**.

### 9.2 Recognition-item recurrence

A recognition item must occur in at least:

- one clear contextual introduction;
- one later listening or reading encounter;
- one contrast, regional note or glossary reminder.

Recognition items must not be required in unprompted speaking tests.

### 9.3 Retrieval scheduling tags

The ledger uses:

- `R0` — introduction;
- `R1` — same chapter;
- `R2` — within two chapters;
- `R3` — later same section;
- `R4` — next section;
- `R5` — distant cumulative return.

Chapter acceptance fails if more than 15% of its new productive items lack scheduled `R2` and `R3` occurrences.

### 9.4 Quality of recurrence

A repeated item counts only when the learner must process its meaning or retrieve it. Reprinting the same vocabulary box does not count.

## 10. Cumulative grammar sequence

Grammar is introduced as an operation inside a sentence engine. Tables summarise patterns after use; they do not replace interaction.

| Section | Required grammar and interactional operations |
|---|---|
| **1** | Independent pronouns; gender where communicatively necessary; zero-copula nominal descriptions; baseline `raa-` state/presence forms; existence and location chunks; demonstratives; basic possession; yes/no and high-frequency content questions; nominal negation; memorised polite imperatives; repetition and clarification requests. |
| **2** | Imperfect/person prefixes for common verbs; present, habitual and context-dependent immediate readings; verb negation `ma …-sh`; positive commands and prohibitions; conjunction; common prepositions; attached possessive forms; basic direct-object suffixes in high-frequency chunks. |
| **3** | Past suffix conjugation; common irregular past forms; past negation; time sequencing; `kaan` for past state/existence; movement complements; short narrative chaining; cause and result connectors. |
| **4** | Future and intention using the reviewed central baseline; `raa-` plus imperfect for ongoing activity where appropriate; ability, desire, obligation and permission; purpose with the reviewed `baash` pattern; invitations, acceptance, refusal and rescheduling. |
| **5** | Numbers and quantity in practical contexts; noun–adjective agreement; comparison; possession expansion; relative `elli`; partitives and containers; polite request softening; object suffix consolidation. |
| **6** | Complement clauses; reasons and explanations; service problems; condition with the reviewed baseline for “if”; sequencing multiple requests; indirect-object patterns; more flexible word order for topic and contrast. |
| **7** | Experience and result expressions; body and health descriptions without false medical precision; fuller clitic combinations where natural; travel narration; “already/still/not yet” distinctions; consequence and contingency. |
| **8** | Past habitual with `kaan` plus imperfect; reported speech and information source; opinion, agreement and disagreement; narrative foreground/background; discourse markers; pragmatic use of code-switching and register shifts. |
| **9** | Counterfactual and conditional patterns; hedging; stance and uncertainty; extended repair; accommodation across regional difference; paraphrase; managing unknown vocabulary; independent multi-turn conversation. |

### 10.1 Early-tense requirement

- Learners must use at least one productive past pattern by **Section 3**.
- Learners must express plans or future intention by **Section 4**.
- Past and future forms must recur in all later sections.

### 10.2 Forms requiring baseline confirmation

The final productive realisation of the following remains `NV-OPEN` until central-urban review:

- future/intention alternatives and their pragmatic differences;
- distribution of `raa-` forms for state, presence and ongoing activity;
- exact negative placement with pronoun suffixes;
- question-particle choices;
- second-person gender marking in specific high-frequency frames;
- relative frequency and acceptability of competing possession constructions.

## 11. Native-validation system

### 11.1 Flags

| Flag | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `NV-OPEN` | Native-speaker review still required. |
| `NV-CENTRAL` | Reviewed for the central urban/Algiers baseline. |
| `NV-REGION` | Reviewed for the named region/community. |
| `NV-PRAG` | Pragmatic, politeness or relationship use reviewed. |
| `NV-CS` | Code-switching naturalness reviewed. |
| `NV-SCRIPT` | Arabic spelling reviewed. |
| `NV-AUDIO` | Natural recording approved against script. |
| `NV-REVISE` | Reviewer found a problem; do not publish as approved. |
| `NV-PASS` | All required review gates complete. |

### 11.2 Minimum review gates

A productive baseline item requires:

- credible attestation or elicitation record;
- one central-urban native review for form and meaning;
- a second independent central-urban review for anchor dialogues and high-recurrence frames;
- pragmatic review where age, gender, hierarchy, intimacy or politeness matters;
- audio review after recording.

A regional recognition item requires:

- documentary evidence or recorded occurrence;
- a reviewer connected to the named speech community;
- cautious geographical wording.

A proposed etymology requires a linguistic source. Native review cannot by itself convert an uncertain origin into fact.

### 11.3 Reviewer record

Store:

- reviewer ID or protected pseudonym;
- self-described place(s) of linguistic socialisation;
- age band;
- languages/varieties used regularly;
- relationship to the represented community;
- review date;
- exact prompt and context;
- judgement: natural / possible but marked / not local / outdated / unclear;
- suggested revision;
- permission level for quotation or audio.

Do not publish personally identifying reviewer metadata without consent.

### 12.2 Required chapter components

1. **Outcome and situation**  
   State what learners will accomplish, with whom and where.

2. **Before you speak**  
   One short activation or prediction prompt; no prerequisite lecture.

3. **Productive vocabulary `[P]`**  
   Arabic, transliteration, flexible English meaning, layer tag, ledger ID and validation flag.

4. **Recognition vocabulary `[R]`**  
   Same fields, plus why recognition matters.

5. **Sentence engine**  
   One to three frames, slots, constraints, negation and pragmatic use.

6. **Accessible dialogue**  
   A believable exchange with a clear purpose and result. Use short turns and controlled density without unnatural completeness.

7. **Understand the exchange**  
   Meaning-first questions about goal, relationship, problem and outcome.

8. **Ordinary-speed dialogue**  
   A natural version that may include reduction, overlap notes or realistic code-switching. It must not introduce an uncontrolled mass of new vocabulary.

9. **Line-by-line text**  
   Arabic script, pedagogical transliteration and natural English.

10. **Pronunciation and rhythm**  
    One primary target; careful phrase, natural phrase, rhythm grouping and learner warning.

11. **Pragmatics**  
    Who can say what to whom; directness, warmth, politeness and likely misreadings.

12. **You may hear…**  
    Bounded regional, social or generational recognition note. Omit rather than invent.

13. **Rooted context**  
    Relevant cultural or historical material connected to the language or situation. Include evidence category and citation.

14. **Practice ladder**
    - notice;
    - choose or match;
    - substitution;
    - cued retrieval;
    - timed response;
    - mixed retrieval with earlier material;
    - repair round.

15. **Listening work**
    - first listen: purpose;
    - second listen: key detail;
    - third listen: wording/rhythm;
    - optional transcript check;
    - one transfer question.

16. **Information-gap or personalised speaking task**  
    Include roles, missing information, non-linguistic outcome, required repair event and self-study adaptation.

17. **Quick review**  
    Five to eight prompts, mostly production from meaning rather than copying.

18. **Audio pack and recording directions**  
    List all clip IDs, speaker requirements and TTS/human decisions.

19. **Sources**  
    Chapter-specific sources with timestamps/pages where possible.

20. **Native-review questions**  
    Concrete questions, not “Is this natural?” alone.

21. **Ledger and recurrence update**  
    New items, demotions/promotions and scheduled `R2–R5` returns.

### 12.3 Dialogue constraints

- The exchange must accomplish a believable purpose.
- No speaker may ask a question whose answer they obviously know solely to display vocabulary unless the setting makes it natural, such as teaching or checking.
- New lexical density should normally remain below one unfamiliar content item per short turn.
- Both speakers need plausible motives.
- Include greetings and closings only when the setting requires them; do not make every exchange ceremonially complete.
- Ordinary-speed versions may be shorter than accessible versions if that is more natural.

## 14. Exercise and section-exit design

### 14.1 Exercise principles

Exercises move from supported recognition to independent interaction. At least half of a chapter’s practice prompts must require retrieval rather than visual copying.

Use:

- meaning-to-Darja recall;
- interlocutor cue → appropriate response;
- substitution with semantic constraints;
- ordering a plausible exchange;
- correcting a misunderstanding;
- choosing between ordinary Darja, MSA and a French switch;
- selective dictation;
- shadowing by thought group;
- information gaps;
- personal choice and explanation.

Translation is permitted when it tests a known communicative meaning. Avoid English sentences engineered to force one-to-one grammar matching.

### 14.2 Information-gap requirements

A genuine information-gap task must include:

- different information for Role A and Role B;
- a reason they cannot simply show each other the sheet;
- a concrete result such as choosing, scheduling, locating or correcting;
- at least one target form that is useful or task-essential;
- a repair route;
- a self-study version using concealed cards, delayed prompts or recorded responses.

### 14.3 Section exit task

Every exit task must:

1. simulate a realistic multi-stage situation;
2. require language from at least three chapters in the section;
3. require language from an earlier section;
4. include a past, present or future contrast once those forms have been introduced;
5. include one clarification or repair event;
6. produce a non-linguistic outcome;
7. have an accessible recorded model and a separate unscripted learner task;
8. avoid requiring recognition-only regional forms.

### 14.4 Exit rubric

Score each category 0–3:

| Category | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Task completion | Not completed | Partly completed | Completed with support | Completed independently |
| Comprehensibility | Usually unclear | Frequent breakdown | Generally understandable | Clear and efficiently repaired |
| Productive language | Minimal | Isolated items | Connected reviewed frames | Flexible combination of frames |
| Interaction | No response management | Limited response | Uses basic clarification | Clarifies, confirms and adapts |
| Time reference | Unavailable | Often confused | Mostly adequate | Controls required contrasts |
| Pragmatic fit | Often inappropriate | Mixed | Generally suitable | Well matched to relationship/setting |

Accent similarity to a native speaker is **not** a scoring category. Comprehensibility, timing and interaction are.

## 15. Evidence, citations and uncertainty

### 15.1 Source hierarchy

Prefer, in order:

1. recorded natural speech with metadata; corpora; published dialect descriptions; peer-reviewed linguistic work;
2. Algerian university and research-centre publications;
3. official legal, archival, museum and cultural-institution sources;
4. specialist dictionaries and professionally reviewed teaching materials;
5. documented native-speaker elicitation;
6. journalism, interviews and reputable public media;
7. social media, forums and informal videos for **occurrence only**.

A social-media example can show that someone used a form. It cannot by itself establish national frequency, etymology, historical causation or universal acceptability.

### 15.2 Linguistic evidence rule

A productive form should normally have:

- one documentary or recorded source plus native review; or
- two independent native elicitations with full context when documentary evidence is unavailable.

High-risk forms—regional claims, humour, insults, intimacy, active code-switching and culturally sensitive language—need stronger contextual review.

### 15.3 Citation style

Use inline author–date citations linked to a source:

```markdown
The corpus distinguishes adapted loans from stretches retaining French phonology
([Harrat et al. 2016](URL), pp. 389–391).
```

For audio or video, add timestamp and speaker/context metadata. For a quotation, add page or timestamp and remain within copyright limits.

Each chapter ends with a **Sources** list containing usable links. Access date is required for changeable web pages.

### 15.4 Claims register

Every researched cultural or historical note classifies its claims as:

- `DOC-EVENT` — documented event;
- `DOC-POLICY` — documented law, policy or institution;
- `ORAL-MEMORY` — attributed personal recollection;
- `COLLECTIVE-NARRATIVE` — documented narrative shared in a defined community or source set;
- `EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION` — course author’s synthesis;
- `UNRESOLVED` — evidence conflict or insufficiency.

### 15.5 Uncertainty language

Use:

- “The available recording documents…”
- “This form is reported for…”
- “Evidence is mixed…”
- “The proposed origin remains uncertain…”
- “Native reviewers differed…”
- “This requires validation before productive use.”

Do not conceal disagreement by choosing the most convenient form.

## 16. Sensitive colonial history

### 16.1 Relevance test

Colonial history may be included only when a documented mechanism connects it to the chapter, such as:

- language hierarchy or education;
- naming and place;
- administration or work;
- oral memory and family transmission;
- neighbourhood organisation or solidarity;
- continuity of food practices;
- music, humour or multilingual appropriation;
- resistance, displacement or migration;
- women’s documented work and organising.

French presence in contemporary Algerian speech may be discussed through documented language contact and policy. Do not imply that every French-derived word entered Darja directly during colonial rule or that present-day speakers use it for one political reason. Research on Algerian language policy documents continuing interaction among Arabic, Tamazight and French across education and public domains, while code-switching research demonstrates present practices rather than one single motivation. ([Mostari 2004](https://www.benjamins.com/catalog/lplp.28.1.04mos); [Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/)). ([benjamins.com](https://www.benjamins.com/catalog/lplp.28.1.04mos?utm_source=openai))

### 16.2 Required separation of evidence

A note must distinguish:

1. what a document establishes;
2. what a named person remembers;
3. what a community narrative reports;
4. what the editor infers.

A family memory is not proof of a national pattern. An official report is not the only possible account. The 2021 Stora report may serve as one commissioned source on memory questions, but sensitive claims require Algerian, independent scholarly or primary evidence alongside it. ([Stora 2021](https://le-souvenir-francais.fr/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/Rapport-Stora-Complet.pdf)). ([le-souvenir-francais.fr](https://le-souvenir-francais.fr/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/Rapport-Stora-Complet.pdf?utm_source=openai))

### 16.3 Prohibitions

Do not:

- use suffering as atmosphere for an unrelated vocabulary lesson;
- invent dialogue for a historical person;
- write composite “authentic memories” without labelling them as fiction;
- present lyrics beyond copyright-compliant excerpts;
- turn food, humour or music into proof of an unsupported historical claim;
- describe Algerians only through colonisation and resistance;
- make inherited multilingual practice sound defective or impure.

### 16.4 Review requirements

Sensitive historical content needs:

- at least two credible sources, preferably including Algerian scholarship or testimony;
- editorial claim classification;
- sensitivity review;
- native review of language;
- human recording when spoken aloud;
- an option for learners to skip distressing listening without losing essential grammar.

## 17. Audio and recording standard

### 17.1 Audio layers

Important utterances should normally have:

1. **Careful phrase** — clear but not syllable-by-syllable distortion.
2. **Natural phrase** — ordinary connected speech.
3. **Shadowing groups** — divided at meaningful rhythm boundaries.
4. **Response or contrast** — the phrase used in interaction or contrasted with a likely misunderstanding.

Shadowing can support comprehensibility, fluency and prosodic control, but evidence for all segmental outcomes is not uniform; it must be combined with retrieval and communicative response work. ([Whitworth & Rose 2025](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/29984475.2025.2546827)). ([tandfonline.com](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/29984475.2025.2546827?utm_source=openai))

### 17.2 TTS use

TTS may prototype:

- isolated vocabulary;
- controlled substitution drills;
- careful review sentences;
- timing and file workflow.

TTS must not be the final authority for pronunciation. It is normally unsuitable for:

- anchor dialogues;
- spontaneous interaction;
- regional comparison;
- meaningful French code-switching;
- humour or teasing;
- hospitality routines whose warmth depends on prosody;
- music-influenced speech;
- oral memory or sensitive history.

Never instruct a generic voice model to “sound Algerian,” “sound Kabyle,” “sound Oranese,” or fabricate another unsupported regional identity.

### 17.3 Human-recording requirement

`human_required: true` for:

- every anchor dialogue;
- every ordinary-speed core dialogue;
- all regional material;
- all sensitive history;
- pragmatically delicate refusal, apology, intimacy or conflict;
- code-switching where the switch itself is pedagogically important;
- laughter, overlap, interruption or marked emotional stance.

### 17.4 Recording direction

Direct speakers by communicative situation, not stereotype:

- relationship and shared knowledge;
- what each speaker wants;
- whether the turn is warm, rushed, hesitant, firm or playful;
- where a pause is motivated;
- whether overlap is permitted;
- whether a French switch is scripted or spontaneous;
- which pronunciation features must remain stable.

Do not direct “more ethnic,” “more rural,” “more Arab,” “more Berber” or similar essentialising performances.

## 20. Editorial acceptance checklist

A chapter is accepted only if all answers are **yes** or an explicit waiver is logged.

### Language

- Does it sound like reviewed Algerian Darja rather than relabelled MSA?
- Is the central baseline internally consistent?
- Are regional forms bounded and cautiously worded?
- Are integrated loans and active switches distinguished?
- Are all productive forms validated or visibly `NV-OPEN`?

### Teaching

- Is there a believable communicative outcome?
- Does the sentence engine combine old and new language?
- Are past/future forms recycled after introduction?
- Does practice move from notice to retrieval to interaction?
- Does the task contain a real gap or personal decision?

### Script and notation

- Are Arabic, transliteration and English aligned?
- Is transliteration consistent with this document?
- Is Arabizi kept separate?
- Are connected-speech notes supported by audio review?

### Culture and history

- Is the cultural material connected to the chapter mechanism?
- Are historical event, memory and interpretation separated?
- Are claims cited?
- Are ordinary pleasure and contemporary life represented alongside difficult history?

### Audio

- Does every clip have a valid ID and manifest record?
- Are human-required clips assigned to humans?
- Are speaker relationship, setting, pace and intent specified?
- Are pronunciation risks and code-switches marked?
- Has the final recording received `NV-AUDIO`?

### Course control

- Are lexical counts reconciled?
- Are recurrence slots scheduled?
- Are obvious duplicates resolved?
- Are later-section handoff notes updated?

## 21. Decisions requiring native or specialist review

The following remain open at foundation stage:

1. Exact central-urban vowel notation for high-frequency function words.
2. Lexical distribution of `q` and `g` in the productive baseline.
3. Preferred Arabic spelling when common practice alternates between ق and ڨ.
4. Baseline future/intention strategy and the pragmatic distribution of alternatives.
5. Detailed distribution of `raa-` state, presence and progressive constructions.
6. Baseline question particles and intonation-only questions.
7. Negative placement around attached object and indirect-object pronouns.
8. Second-person gender distinctions in frequent frames.
9. Naturalness and social distribution of proposed productive French switches.
10. Pragmatic force of greetings, refusals, hospitality formulas, teasing and terms of address.
11. All region-labelled pronunciation and vocabulary notes.
12. Any proposed Amazigh etymology or structural influence not already established by specialist research.
13. Arabic-script treatment of newly selected integrated loans.
14. Ordinary-speed reductions and rhythm groups in anchor dialogues.
15. Historical oral-memory scripts and their permissions.

No open decision may be silently converted into a universal rule by a chapter writer.
