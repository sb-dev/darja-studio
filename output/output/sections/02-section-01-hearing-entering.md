# Section 1 — Hearing and entering the language

Algerian Darja becomes easier to enter when you stop waiting to understand every sound before responding. This section builds a first conversation engine: hear compact rhythm groups, greet people, identify yourself, say where you are from and repair misunderstandings. The productive baseline is broadly central urban and associated mainly with Algiers and its periphery; bounded notes will help you recognise some other Algerian forms without treating any one variety as universal.

Arabic script supports recognition from the beginning, but it does not delay speaking. Use the audio, course transliteration and repair language actively. All productive forms in this section remain subject to the stated native-review gates.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- say who you are;
- check whether you have reached the expected person;
- say that you are here;
- say that you did not understand;
- request a slower repetition;
- hear the difference between careful and ordinary connected speech.

### Situation

You have arrived at a busy meeting point for a language exchange. You call the contact number you were given. Traffic and other voices make one answer hard to catch, so you confirm the person’s name and ask for a slower repetition.

The goal is not perfect pronunciation. The goal is to remain in the conversation.

## 6. Accessible dialogue

### Purpose and setting

Noura calls Samir from a noisy public entrance. They have not met before. She confirms his identity, reports that she has arrived and repairs one unclear turn. The tone is neutral-warm and cooperative; no French switch is scripted.

### Dialogue

**1. نورا**

- **Arabic:** سلام. نتا سمير؟
- **Transliteration:** `salaam. nta Smiir?`
- **English:** Hi. Are you Samir?

**2. سمير**

- **Arabic:** إيه. نتي نورا؟
- **Transliteration:** `iih. nti Nuura?`
- **English:** Yes. Are you Noura?

**3. نورا**

- **Arabic:** إيه. أنا نورا. راني هنا.
- **Transliteration:** `iih. ana Nuura. raani hna.`
- **English:** Yes. I’m Noura. I’m here.

**4. سمير**

- **Arabic:** أنا سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m Samir.

**5. نورا**

- **Arabic:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
- **English:** Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat more slowly.

**6. سمير**

- **Arabic:** أنا / سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana / Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m / Samir.

**7. نورا**

- **Arabic:** إيه.
- **Transliteration:** `iih.`
- **English:** Right.

### Result

The speakers confirm the correct contact. Noura does not pretend to understand: she repairs the problem and the call continues.

- `raani hna` forms one close rhythm group;
- `ma fhemt-sh` is heard as one compact repair unit;

- the names carry the principal identifying beat.

These reductions and beat placements are rehearsal proposals, not universal descriptions. They require `NV-AUDIO` confirmation.

## 9. What you are hearing

### 9.1 Darja is not defective Modern Standard Arabic

Algerian Darja differs from Modern Standard Arabic in sound patterns, morphology, vocabulary and syntax. Treating it as MSA pronounced carelessly misrepresents an ordinary language of interaction. Published resources also describe substantial internal variation and the absence of one universally settled everyday spelling system (Saadane & Habash 2015, 69–75; Harrat et al. 2016, 384–390).

`EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`: In this course, **oral and adaptive** means suited to rapid interaction, local relationships and multilingual life. It does not mean broken, careless or grammatically empty.

### 9.2 Compact speech still contains vowels

Maghrebi speech commonly permits compact consonant sequences and reduced short vowels. Work on Algerian orthography also describes vowel shortening and differences between written and surface forms (Saadane & Habash 2015, 74–75).

- **Arabic:** ما فهمتش.
- **Transliteration:** `ma fhemt-sh.`
- **English:** I didn’t understand.

Do not expand the middle into a series of full English-like vowels:

```text
ma | fhemt | sh
     ^ main beat proposed for review
```

The `e` is a short teaching vowel. Its precise quality must follow reviewed audio.

### 9.3 Words connect into rhythm groups

Ordinary speech is organised around meaning and emphasis, not the spaces printed on a page.

```text
Careful:  raani / hna
Connected target: raani_hna

Careful:  ma / fhemt / sh
Connected target: ma_fhemt-sh
```

The underscore is a pronunciation mark meaning “keep these close.” It is not Arabic punctuation or a new spelling.

### 9.4 Careful and ordinary speech are both real

A cooperative speaker may separate a phrase after a repair request. That careful response is not fake; it is an ordinary conversational adjustment.

**First attempt**

- **Arabic:** أنا سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m Samir.

**Repair request**

- **Arabic:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
- **English:** Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat more slowly.

**Careful response**

- **Arabic:** أنا / سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana / Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m / Samir.

Your aim is to understand both versions, not to force every speaker to remain at classroom speed.

## 10. Essential consonants: a first map

You do not need to master the whole sound system today. Preserve each symbol and improve it gradually.

| Symbol | Arabic | Beginner direction | Chapter example | Common risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `ḥ` | ح | Strong voiceless breath made lower in the throat than English `h`. | `smaḥ-li` — سمحلي | Replacing it entirely with a light `h`. |
| `ʿ` | ع | A gentle, brief narrowing in the throat. | `ʿaawed` — عاود | Deleting it or replacing it with `g` or `r`. |
| `h` | ه | Ordinary breathy `h`. | `hna` — هنا | Adding a large vowel before `hn-`. |
| `q` | ق | A stop farther back than `k`; its realisation varies by item and speaker. | `qahwa` — قهوة `[R]` | Applying one automatic “Algerian” conversion rule. |
| `kh` | خ | Friction like Scottish *loch* or German *Bach*. | No productive item yet. | Saying separate `k` and `h`. |
| `gh` | غ | The voiced counterpart of `kh`. | No productive item yet. | Replacing it automatically with hard `g`. |
| `zh` | often ج where reviewed | The sound in English *measure*. | No productive item yet. | Assuming every Algerian ج has one pronunciation. |
| `ṭ ḍ ṣ ẓ` | ط ض ص ظ | Backed or “emphatic” consonants that can colour nearby vowels. | No productive item yet. | Treating the distinction as spelling only. |
| doubled consonant | often ّ | Hold the consonant longer without adding a syllable. | No productive contrast yet. | Inserting a vowel between two copies. |

The course represents gemination with doubled transliteration consonants and, where useful, Arabic shadda. Algerian descriptions treat shadda as consonant length, not an extra syllable (Harrat et al. 2016, 389–390).

### Cluster practice

Keep each item compact:

1. `هنا — hna — here`
2. `سمحلي — smaḥ-li — excuse me; sorry`
3. `فهمت — fhemt — understood`, used here only inside the fixed negative expression

```text
h...na       → hna
s...maḥ-li   → smaḥ-li
f...hemt     → fhemt
```

Do not race. Compact does not mean tense.

## 11. Arabic script, course transliteration and Arabizi

### 11.1 Three systems, three jobs

| System | Example | Job |
|---|---|---|
| Pedagogical Arabic script | ما فهمتش | Builds recognition using stable course conventions. |
| Course transliteration | `ma fhemt-sh` | Shows the teaching pronunciation, useful boundaries and consonant distinctions. |
| Informal Arabizi | attested forms include `mafhemtch` | Variable user-created digital writing; not the course pronunciation key. |

Algerian corpora contain Arabic script, Latin script and mixed writing with considerable spelling variation rather than one fixed public Arabizi standard (Cotterell et al. 2014; Touileb & Barnes 2021).

### 11.2 Reading the Arabic line

Arabic runs from right to left, and short vowels are normally omitted. Audio and transliteration therefore remain necessary.

- **Arabic:** راني هنا.
- **Transliteration:** `raani hna.`
- **English:** I’m here.

The Arabic line does not separately display every pronunciation detail represented by the course transliteration.

### 11.3 Reading course transliteration

- `aa`, `ii`, `uu` show longer vowels.
- `e` is a short central or reduced teaching vowel.
- `ḥ` and `ʿ` preserve consonants absent from ordinary English spelling.
- A hyphen exposes a useful teaching boundary: `smaḥ-li`, `ma …-sh`.
- An underscore is used only in rhythm work: `raani_hna`.

### 11.4 Arabizi recognition preview

In many Arabizi traditions, including documented Algerian material, `3` may represent ع and `7` may represent ح. Actual choices and spellings vary by writer and setting (Guellil et al. 2018; Touileb & Barnes 2021).

| Arabizi sign `[R]` | Arabic | Course symbol | Chapter component |
|---|---|---|---|
| `3` | ع | `ʿ` | `ʿaawed` — عاود |
| `7` | ح | `ḥ` | `smaḥ-li` — سمحلي |

Do not mechanically rewrite course phrases with numerals. Naturally occurring or elicited Arabizi forms must be recorded as variable usage, not invented through automatic conversion.

## 12. Pragmatics: repair keeps you involved

A beginner who stays silent after missing a phrase may accidentally signal agreement or understanding. Repair keeps both people coordinated.

- **Arabic:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
- **English:** Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat more slowly.

### Tone and relationship

- Use a calm or slightly apologetic tone with a stranger.
- `smaḥ-li` softens the direct request `ʿaawed b-shwiya`.
- A bare, sharply delivered `ʿaawed!` may sound abrupt depending on the relationship, tone and locality; it is not the beginner default.
- `laa` is not inherently rude, but a loud or prolonged delivery can sound confrontational.
- `iih` can confirm identity, but its pronunciation and contrast with other acknowledgements require native review.
- Do not imitate speed by swallowing every sound. If you become unclear, return to the careful phrase and reconnect it gradually.

## 13. You may hear… different pronunciations of ق

**Recognition only: this is not an accent-conversion rule.**

The provisional course carrier is:

- **Arabic:** قهوة
- **Transliteration:** `qahwa`
- **English:** coffee

Research describes several Algerian reflexes of historical ق, including `q`, `g`, a glottal stop and `k`, distributed unevenly across locations, speakers and lexical items. Saadane and Habash report broad examples involving Algiers, Annaba, Sétif, Tlemcen and specified northern localities, but such descriptions do not authorise automatic conversion of every word (2015, 71, 75–76).

| Status | Possible heard form | Bounded interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Provisional carrier | `qahwa` | Requires central-urban review before productive use. |
| Recognition | `gahwa` | You may hear `g` from speakers or communities using it in this item. |
| Recognition | `’ahwa` | You may hear a glottal onset in some local speech; this has been reported for Tlemcen. |
| Recognition | `kahwa` | You may hear `k` in some northern varieties documented for this reflex. |

**Learner action:** notice that these may be related forms. Do not turn every `q` into `g`, `’` or `k`.

No generic TTS regional comparison is authorised. Any future comparison requires appropriately profiled human speakers and community-connected review.

## 14. Rooted context: an oral language can also be written

**Claim classes:** `DOC-LINGUISTIC`, `EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`.

Darja has long been centred on oral interaction, while formal Arabic has occupied many formal written domains. This division does not make everyday speech structurally deficient. Algerian research describes differences from MSA at every major linguistic level and proposes specialised orthographic conventions because everyday writing is variable—not because it is absent (Saadane & Habash 2015, 69–75).

Digital communication has made Algerian speech particularly visible in Arabic script, Latin script and mixed forms. Corpora assembled from online material document this adaptability, multilingual content and inconsistent spelling (Cotterell et al. 2014; Touileb & Barnes 2021).

`EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`: Arabic script, course transliteration and Arabizi are tools with different purposes. None should be used to decide whether a speaker’s Darja is “pure.”

Colonial language history is not expanded here because pronunciation alone does not provide a responsible mechanism for discussing it. Chapter 3 connects names and places to documented colonial administration; Chapter 4 addresses multilingual repertoire and language hierarchy with explicit evidence limits.

## 17. Information gap — Find the correct contact

### Outcome

### Role A: caller

- expected contact: **Samir**;

- Adam;

## 20. Native-review questions

### Productive language and pragmatics

1. Is `إيه — iih` a suitable uncomplicated productive “yes” for this baseline? Record its natural vowel and compare it with `آه — aah` and other acknowledgements without automatically promoting them.
2. Is `سمحلي — smaḥ-li` the preferred stable course spelling, or should `سماحلي` or another form be used for the intended pronunciation?
3. Does `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya` sound suitably polite between unfamiliar adults?
4. Is `b-shwiya` natural for “more slowly” here, or does it primarily suggest “gently/a little” without additional wording?
5. Is `raani hna` natural on arrival at a mutually known meeting point, and what readings arise outside that context?
6. Are `nta` and `nti` suitable productive singular forms? Record any ordinary reductions without replacing the careful teaching forms prematurely.
7. Are `nta Smiir?` and `nti Nuura?` natural with intonation alone in this phone context? Compare them with `waash` versions.
8. Does `laa. ana Liina.` sound like a natural correction, or is a discourse marker normally expected?

### Pronunciation and rhythm

9. Record careful and ordinary `ma fhemt-sh`. Identify the vowel quality in `fhemt`, any assimilation and the main beat.
10. Does `ma_fhemt-sh` support accurate rhythm, or does it imply a misleading boundary?
11. Record careful and ordinary `raani hna`; note linking, shortening or consonant adjustment.
12. In this exact deictic context, do reviewers prefer `hna`, `hnaaya` or another form? Keep alternatives distinct by meaning and deixis.
13. Is any support vowel audible before the clusters in `smaḥ-li`, `fhemt` or `hna`?
14. Where does the principal beat fall in polite `ʿaawed b-shwiya`, and how does impatient prosody differ?
15. Does `ʿaawed` accurately represent the first vowel, vowel length and realisation of ع?

# Chapter 2 — Greeting people naturally

> **Validation notice:** Productive language in this chapter follows a proposed central urban baseline associated mainly with Algiers and its close urban periphery. Core forms are documented, but exact vowels, ordinary-speed reductions and pragmatic range still require two independent central-urban reviews. Threshold hospitality, respectful address, repetition and leave-taking also require pragmatic review.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- greet one person or several people and answer immediately;
- ask and answer a brief “How are you?” question;
- welcome a guest and invite them inside;
- adjust key endings for one man, one woman or a group;
- greet an older person respectfully without guessing an unsuitable title;
- close a short visit with a natural farewell.

### Realistic situation

You have arranged to visit your friend Yasmine. Her mother, Nadia, opens the door. You greet her, exchange a short wellbeing check and wait for her invitation before entering. Later, you say goodbye to the household.

## 2. Before you speak

You hear a host say:

> **مرحبا بيك، تفضّلي.**  
> `marḥba bik, tfaḍḍli.`

Without translating word by word, decide:

1. Is the speaker probably the host or the guest?
2. Is the guest one man, one woman or a group?
3. Which expression welcomes the guest? Which one moves the visit forward?

You will check your answers after learning the doorway frame.

## 5. Sentence engine

### Frame C02-F01 — Open and answer

- **Purpose:** establish contact when arriving or entering.
- **Frame:** `السّلام عليكم ← وعليكم السّلام`
- **Transliteration:** `s-slaam ʿlikom ← wa ʿlikom s-slaam`
- **Natural English:** “Hello.” ← “Hello.”
- **Beginner rule:** answer the opening before moving to the main business.

> **السّلام عليكم.**  
> `s-slaam ʿlikom.`  
> “Hello.”
>
> **وعليكم السّلام.**  
> `wa ʿlikom s-slaam.`  
> “Hello.”

At a doorway, the response can flow directly into the welcome:

> **وعليكم السّلام، مرحبا بيك.**  
> `wa ʿlikom s-slaam, marḥba bik.`  
> “Hello—welcome.”

### Frame C02-F02 — Check wellbeing and return the question

- **Purpose:** exchange a brief social wellbeing check.
- **Frame:** `واش راك/راكي/راكم؟ ← لاباس، الحمد لله. ونتا/نتي/نتوما؟`
- **Transliteration:** `waash raak/raaki/raakom? ← la baas, l-ḥamdullah. u nta/nti/ntuma?`
- **Natural English:** “How are you?” ← “Fine, thank God. And you?”
- **Pragmatics:** often a social check rather than a request for a detailed medical report. Close relationships can support a fuller answer.

| Addressee | Question | Return question |
|---|---|---|
| one man | **واش راك؟** — `waash raak?` | **ونتا؟** — `u nta?` |
| one woman | **واش راكي؟** — `waash raaki?` | **ونتي؟** — `u nti?` |
| group | **واش راكم؟** — `waash raakom?` | **ونتوما؟** — `u ntuma?` |

A shorter exchange is:

> **لاباس؟**  
> `la baas?`  
> “All good?”
>
> **لاباس، الحمد لله.**  
> `la baas, l-ḥamdullah.`  
> “Fine, thank God.”

Research on Algerian conversational examples discusses `waash raak?` as a phatic, contact-maintaining question. That does not make every answer empty; relationship and context still matter ([Bourahla 2013, p. 129](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/23/20/3/3439)).

### Frame C02-F03 — Welcome and move through the doorway

- **Purpose:** welcome a guest and invite them inside.
- **Frame:** `مرحبا بيك/بيكم. تفضّل/تفضّلي/تفضّلوا.`
- **Transliteration:** `marḥba bik/bikom. tfaḍḍal/tfaḍḍli/tfaḍḍlu.`
- **Natural English:** “Welcome. Come in.”
- **Pragmatics:** the host gives the invitation. A guest should not use `tfaḍḍal` to command the host to enter.

| Guest | Welcome | Invitation |
|---|---|---|
| one man | **مرحبا بيك.** — `marḥba bik.` | **تفضّل.** — `tfaḍḍal.` |
| one woman | **مرحبا بيك.** — `marḥba bik.` | **تفضّلي.** — `tfaḍḍli.` |
| group | **مرحبا بيكم.** — `marḥba bikom.` | **تفضّلوا.** — `tfaḍḍlu.` |

One warm repetition may reinforce the welcome:

> **مرحبا بيك، مرحبا.**  
> `marḥba bik, marḥba.`  
> “You’re very welcome.”

> **تفضّلي، تفضّلي.**  
> `tfaḍḍli, tfaḍḍli.`  
> “Come in, come in.”

The translations show the social effect rather than counting every repeated word. Timing, pitch and relationship require human recording and pragmatic review.

**Purpose:** Lina arrives for a planned visit. Nadia, Yasmine’s mother, greets her and makes it clear that she may enter.

- **Nadia:** older adult host and the mother of Lina’s friend.

1. **Lina:** **السّلام عليكم.**  
   `s-slaam ʿlikom.`  
   “Hello.”

2. **Nadia:** **وعليكم السّلام. مرحبا بيك، لاباس؟**  
   `wa ʿlikom s-slaam. marḥba bik, la baas?`  
   “Hello. Welcome—are you well?”

3. **Lina:** **لاباس، الحمد لله. ونتي؟**

Lina now enters; no extra verbal response is required in the beginner script. Reviewers should confirm whether a locally preferred acknowledgement should be introduced later rather than adding an artificial “thank you” here.

6. **Nadia:** **بالسلامة.**

**Answers:** 1. Lina. 2. Line 2. 3. Nadia uses `tfaḍḍli`; Lina’s `u nti?` also addresses a woman. 4. No. 5. Lina enters. 6. `tfaḍḍli, tfaḍḍli`.

## 8. Pronunciation and rhythm

### Keep social phrases together

| Careful phrase | Natural grouping | Main learner risk |
|---|---|---|
| `s-slaam ʿlikom` | `s-slaam_ʿlikom` | separating every written syllable |
| `wa ʿlikom s-slaam` | `wa_ʿlikom_s-slaam` | making the short `wa` too heavy |
| `waash raak?` | `waash_raak?` | adding English “do” or breaking the phrase in two |
| `marḥba bik` | `marḥba_bik` | replacing ح with a very weak English `h` |
| `b-slaama` | `b-slaama` | inserting extra vowels: *bə-sə-laama* |

### The sound `ḥ`

In **مرحبا** `marḥba` and **الحمد لله** `l-ḥamdullah`, `ḥ` represents **ح**. It is stronger and produced farther back than English `h`. Aim for steady airflow; do not squeeze painfully.

### The doubled `ḍḍ`

In **تفضّل** `tfaḍḍal`, hold the middle consonant briefly:

- avoid a quick single consonant: `tfa-ḍal`;
- aim toward `tfaḍ-ḍal`.

The exact emphatic quality and duration must follow human audio.

### Shadowing sequence

1. Careful: `marḥba bik. tfaḍḍli.`
2. Natural grouping: `marḥba_bik / tfaḍḍli.`
3. Warm repetition: `marḥba_bik / tfaḍḍli_tfaḍḍli.`
4. Contrast: hear a host address a man, a woman and a group; point to the correct picture before repeating.

## 9. Pragmatics and social guidance

### 9.1 Answer the opening first

When someone opens with **السّلام عليكم**, respond with **وعليكم السّلام** before moving to the purpose of the visit. Greeting sequences are interactional openings, not decorative vocabulary. Algerian research treats them as socially meaningful sequences and positive-politeness resources ([*Revue Algérienne des Sciences Humaines et Sociales* 2024, pp. 186–196](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/259/12/2/259305)).

### 9.2 Warmth is more than repetition

A host may repeat **مرحبا** or **تفضّلي** to reinforce a welcome. More repetition is not automatically warmer: pitch, speed, gesture, relationship and whether the host is busy all matter. Copy an approved human model rather than repeating mechanically.

### 9.3 Respect is not one magic title

With an older person, a clear greeting, immediate response, calm pacing and appropriate wellbeing question are safer beginner strategies than guessing a title. Research in the extreme west of Algeria documents varied choices—including kin terms, pilgrimage titles, French forms and title avoidance—and warns that preferences differ among groups ([Boumedini 2021, §§4.2.2–5](https://aleph.edinum.org/3718?lang=en)).

Do not assume:

- every older man should be called `l-ḥaajj`;
- every older woman should be called `l-ḥaajja` or `khaalti`;
- plural “you” automatically creates a respectful singular form like French *vous*;
- first-name use carries the same meaning in every family.

The cited western study must not be projected automatically onto Algiers or all Algeria.

### 9.4 Entering a home

At the threshold:

1. greet the people present;
2. allow the host to make the invitation clear;
3. follow household cues rather than assuming one national rule about footwear, seating or physical greetings;
4. use group forms when several guests are being welcomed together.

### 9.5 Physical greetings

This chapter teaches verbal language only. Handshakes, cheek-kissing and other contact vary with relationship, gender, family preference, religious practice and personal comfort. A smile, nod and spoken greeting provide a safe non-contact option. Never force contact in order to appear “authentic.”

### 9.6 French greeting switches

You may hear `bonjour`, `bonsoir` or `salut` inside an otherwise Darja interaction. This is active French code-switching, not evidence that French is required or preferred by every Algerian. Algerian Arabic–French switching is well documented, but its density and function vary across speakers and settings ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf)).

## 11. Rooted context: greeting as relationship work

**Claim type: editorial synthesis based on documented conversational evidence.**

The wellbeing question in this chapter does more than request information. It acknowledges the other person and creates a bridge into the visit. Algerian conversational examples show that **واش راك؟** can maintain social contact; deliberately hostile or over-literal responses can disrupt that contact ([Bourahla 2013, p. 129](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/23/20/3/3439)).

That is why the course teaches a sequence rather than an isolated phrase:

> greeting → response → wellbeing check → returned question → welcome or invitation

French greetings are included here because documented multilingual practice makes them relevant to recognition. No colonial-history insert is added merely because French occurs: historical language hierarchy belongs where policy, education or speaker experience can be connected to the communicative topic with appropriate evidence.

# Chapter 3 — Who I am and where I come from

> **Native-review status:** All productive Darja remains **`NV-OPEN`** pending two independent reviews by speakers familiar with ordinary central-urban Algiers usage. The verbs in this chapter are memorised first- and second-person chunks, not a full present-tense system.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- give and ask a name;
- say where you come from and where you live now;
- state Algerian identity, when relevant, with gender agreement;
- say which languages you speak or understand;
- describe limited understanding without claiming fluency;
- correct someone who confuses origin with current residence.

### Realistic situation

You are registering for a neighbourhood language exchange in Algiers. A volunteer needs your name, origin, current residence and languages so that they can choose a suitable partner. You then meet that partner and confirm the information.

The productive exchange stays in Darja. French words that may appear on a form are included for recognition only.

---

## 2. Before you speak

When someone asks where you are from, you might answer with your birthplace, nationality, hometown, family origin or another place of belonging. That answer may differ from where you live now.

In this chapter:

- **`men + place`** gives the origin you choose to state;
- **`neskon fi + place`** gives your current residence.

You never have to disclose ancestry, ethnicity or family history that you would rather keep private.

## 5. Sentence engine

### Frame C03-F01 — Ask and give a name

**Arabic:** `واش اسمك؟ — أنا سمي [الاسم].`  
**Transliteration:** `waash smek? — ana smi [NAME].`  
**Meaning:** “What’s your name?” — “My name’s [name].”

- The name fills one open slot.
- `smi [name]` without `ana` may be heard in a shorter answer.
- In a formal institutional encounter, speakers may choose a different or softened question. That register is deferred.

**Examples**

- `أنا سمي سمير.` — `ana smi Smiir.` — “My name’s Samir.”
- `أنا سمي نور.` — `ana smi Nuur.` — “My name’s Nour.”

### Frame C03-F02 — Separate origin from residence

**Arabic:** `أنا من [المكان]، نسكن في [المكان].`  
**Transliteration:** `ana men [PLACE], neskon fi [PLACE].`  
**Meaning:** “I’m from [place], and I live in [place].”

Ask a man:

- `نتا من وين؟` — `nta men wiin?` — “Where are you from?”
- `وين تسكن؟` — `wiin tesken?` — “Where do you live?”

Ask a woman:

- `نتي من وين؟` — `nti men wiin?`
- `وين تسكني؟` — `wiin teskni?`

**Examples**

- `أنا من وهران، نسكن في العاصمة.`  
  `ana men Wahran, neskon fi l-ʿaasma.`  
  “I’m from Oran, and I live in Algiers.”

- `أنا من ميريكان، نسكن في العاصمة.`  
  `ana men Miriikaan, neskon fi l-ʿaasma.`  
  “I’m from the United States, and I live in Algiers.”

`Miriikaan` is a provisional slot pronunciation, not counted vocabulary.

### Frame C03-F03 — State language ability modestly

**Arabic:** `نهدر [اللغة]. نفهم [اللغة] شوية.`  
**Transliteration:** `nahder [LANGUAGE]. nefhem [LANGUAGE] shwiya.`  
**Meaning:** “I speak [language]. I understand a little [language].”

Ask a man:

- `واش تهدر الدارجة؟` — `waash tahder d-daarja?`
- `واش تفهم الدارجة؟` — `waash tefhem d-daarja?`

Ask a woman:

- `واش تهدري الدارجة؟` — `waash tahdri d-daarja?`
- `واش تفهمي الدارجة؟` — `waash tefhmi d-daarja?`

**Examples**

- `نهدر الإنجليزية، ونفهم الدارجة شوية.`  
  `nahder l-ingliiziya, w nefhem d-daarja shwiya.`  
  “I speak English, and I understand a little Darja.”

- `نهدر الدارجة والفرانسي.`  
  `nahder d-daarja w l-fronsis.`  
  “I speak Darja and French.”

Use recycled `ma fhemt-sh` for a particular message you did not understand. A general sentence meaning “I do not speak X” is deferred.

## 8. Pronunciation and rhythm

### Main target: compact verb chunks

Maghrebi speech commonly permits substantial short-vowel reduction or deletion. Follow reviewed audio rather than restoring vowels from formal Arabic spelling ([Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf)).

#### Careful phrase

`ana men Wahran / neskon fi l-ʿaasma.`

Keep `neskon` compact; do not turn it into a slow formal-Arabic-style sequence.

#### Natural phrase

`ana_men_Wahran / neskon_fi_l-ʿaasma.`

The main beats fall on the places:

`ana men **WAHRAN** / neskon fi l-**ʿAASMA**.`

#### Shadowing groups

- `ana men Wahran`
- `neskon fi l-ʿaasma`
- the complete line without an extra pause after `ana`

#### Meaning contrast

- `nta men wiin?` → origin
- `wiin tesken?` → current residence

### `ḥ`, `ʿ`, doubled consonants and `ɣ`

- `ḥ` in recycled `mliiḥ` is stronger than English *h*.
- Keep `ʿ` visible in `l-ʿaasma`, even if your first approximation is imperfect.
- In `ṣaḥḥa`, hold `ḥḥ` slightly longer; do not insert a full vowel between the consonants.
- `ɣ` in `tamaaziɣt` represents a sound similar to a voiced French or Northumbrian *r*. Copy a speaker who actually uses the word; do not let generic TTS invent it.

Do not pronounce `d-daarja` as English “dar-JEE-uh.” Its rhythm and article assimilation require reviewed audio.

## 9. Pragmatics

### Origin is not one simple fact

`nta/nti men wiin?` may request birthplace, nationality, hometown, family origin or a place of belonging. Context helps, but the person chooses the relevant level.

- `ana men Wahran` — “I’m from Oran.”
- `ana dzayri/dzayriya` — “I’m Algerian.”
- `neskon fi l-ʿaasma` — “I live in Algiers.”

Do not repeatedly question someone because their name, accent or appearance does not match your expectations. This matters especially where birthplace, citizenship, family history and present home differ.

### Regional, national and Amazigh identities

These identities can overlap. Do not infer that someone speaks Tamazight from a surname, birthplace or appearance. Ask about language ability or follow the person’s own description. `tamaaziɣt` is an umbrella language name here; it does not imply that Kabyle, Chaoui, Mozabite and other varieties are interchangeable.

### Limited ability

`nefhem shwiya` is useful and modest. It signals that some communication is possible without promising fluent speech. Your partner can then slow down, repeat or use the repair language from Chapters 1 and 4.

### “Arabic” may need clarification

`l-ʿarbiya` can be broad. When necessary, distinguish:

- `d-daarja` — ordinary spoken Darja;
- `l-fuṣḥa` — formal or Modern Standard Arabic, recognition only here.

## 11. Rooted context — names, places and administrative identity

### Why this belongs here

This chapter asks you to give a name and place. In Algeria, the forms used for names and places on identity documents, maps and signs were shaped partly by colonial registration and renaming and by later post-independence standardisation. The connection here is administrative and linguistic, not decorative.

### Documented policy and events

**Civil registration — `DOC-POLICY`.** The colonial law of **23 March 1882** generalised a hereditary patronymic civil-status system for people classified by the administration as indigenous Muslim Algerians. Ouerdia Yermeche describes this administrative system as a break in writing from naming practices that could identify people through filiation, kin groups and other locally meaningful designations ([Yermeche, “L’état civil algérien,” pp. 489–497](https://books.openedition.org/irmc/1492)).

**Transcription — `DOC-EVENT`.** Yermeche documents multiple Latin-script spellings of the same names and argues that colonial transcription frequently represented Arabic and Amazigh consonants and structures poorly. Her analysis distinguishes a person’s continuing oral designation from the fixed patronym used in administration.

**Urban and place names — `DOC-POLICY`.** CRASC research describes colonial naming of streets and neighbourhoods, followed after independence by laws and decrees intended to reconstruct that naming landscape. Official changes did not always erase locally retained names or eliminate competing spellings ([CRASC, “Odonymy in Algeria”](https://crasc.dz/en/research-project/lodonymie-en-algerie-dysfonctionnements-de-la-denomination-au-niveau-officiel-et-societal)).

**Post-independence standardisation — `DOC-POLICY`.** Commune-name changes began in the 1960s. A decree of **7 March 1981** addressed national-language transcription of names. Algerian onomastics scholarship has criticised approaches that inadequately represent Algeria’s plural linguistic history or diverge from local usage ([Benramdane, “Algérianité et onomastique”](https://www.insaniyat.crasc.dz/ar/article/algerianite-et-onomastique-penser-le-changement-une-question-de-noms-propres)). The exact decree wording and associated dates must be checked against the *Journal officiel* before publication.

### Interpretation, clearly labelled

**`EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`, based on Algerian scholarship:** A difference between a family’s spoken pronunciation and a passport spelling is not automatically a personal error. It may reflect older administrative transcription, later standardisation, migration paperwork or ordinary movement among Arabic, Tamazight and Latin scripts.

Yermeche interprets parts of colonial civil registration as francisation and symbolic violence. That is a documented scholarly interpretation, not the wording of the 1882 law. The evidence does not show that every unusual modern spelling was deliberately imposed or has the same history.

### Practical consequence

- Pronounce a person’s name as they pronounce it.
- Do not “correct” a surname to fit your guess about Arabic or Tamazight.
- A street or town may have an official name, an older locally retained name or competing spellings.
- When clarity matters, ask for repetition or spelling rather than offering an unsolicited etymology.

**Optional reflection:** Have you ever had your name respelled, shortened or mispronounced by an institution? You may skip this question without losing any language practice.

Give yourself three seconds per cue:

- name?
- from where?
- live where?
- speak English?
- understand Darja?

Use one greeting, one new identity answer and one earlier repair phrase.

Your partner deliberately confuses the two facts.

- You: `ana men Wahran, neskon fi l-ʿaasma.`
- Partner: “So you’re from Algiers?”
- You: `ana men **Wahran** / neskon fi **l-ʿaasma**.`

Do not add a new negation pattern. Correct the record by restating both positive facts with clear contrastive stress.

# Chapter 4 — Understanding and repairing conversation

> **Native-review status:** All new productive forms remain `NV-OPEN`. Reviewers must confirm central-Algiers/periphery pronunciation, politeness, Arabic spelling and ordinary-speed reductions before publication. Forms already introduced in Chapters 1–3 retain their earlier review flags.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- say that you did not understand;
- ask someone to repeat or speak more slowly;
- ask what a word or sentence means;
- ask how to say something in Darja;
- correct a mistaken name or detail without sounding confrontational;
- recognise a brief French switch used to clarify or accommodate.

**Situation:** You are signing in at a neighbourhood language exchange. The organiser mishears your name and later tries French. You repair both problems instead of pretending to understand.

Repair is not failure. Research on Algerian bilingual interaction documents repetition, questioning, reformulation and code-switching among the resources speakers use when communication breaks down. The available studies do not establish one universal strategy for every Algerian speaker or setting ([Belgrimet 2020, 459–467](https://ijels.com/detail/a-conversation-analysis-of-repair-strategies-in-communication-breakdowns-a-case-study-of-algerian-bilingual-students/)).

## 2. The repair sequence

When you lose the thread, use this sequence:

> **signal the problem → request specific help → listen again → confirm or correct**

For example:

> سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.  
> `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`  
> Sorry, I didn’t understand. Say it again more slowly.

The first three chunks are recycled from Chapter 1. This chapter makes them part of a larger repair system.

`b-shwiya` is stored as a reusable attachment to the already productive item `shwiya`, not as another unique lexical item. Likewise, positive `fhemt` is an inflected form linked to `ma fhemt-sh`, not a separate vocabulary entry.

Written dialogue material in the University of Tlemcen repository contains contextual examples including **ما فهمتش واش تقصد؟**, **عاود**, **ما سمعتش مليح** and **قصدي**. This establishes occurrence in the represented dramatic text, not national frequency or a central-Algiers preference ([University of Tlemcen repository, 115–116](https://dspace.univ-tlemcen.dz/bitstreams/8ceaecb6-5154-4157-9e1b-9c3ba51e4ec3/download)). A classroom request study also reports **من فضلك**, but its population and setting do not establish identical use in every informal encounter ([Hafsi 2022](https://doaj.org/article/20fb8efa7bac44afb514022ac3691d91)).

### Meaning limits

- `ma fhemt-sh` can mean “I didn’t understand” or “I didn’t catch that.” It does not inherently blame the speaker.
- `b-shwiya` is taught inside a repetition request, not as a universal translation of English *slowly*.
- `yaʿni` can explain another person’s words or reformulate your own.
- `qṣdi` introduces what **you intended**. Do not insert it every time English uses *I mean*.
- `maashi` corrects a name, identification or description. The wider negation system comes later.

## 5. Sentence engine

### Frame C04-F01 — Signal non-understanding and request help

**Skeleton**

> سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية، من فضلك.  
> `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed [b-shwiya], [men faḍlek].`  
> Sorry, I didn’t understand. Say it again [more slowly], [please].

Use one or more softeners according to the relationship. You do not need to stack all of them mechanically.

**Examples**

1. سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود، من فضلك.  
   `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed, men faḍlek.`  
   Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat, please.

2. ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.  
   `ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`  
   I didn’t get that. Say it again more slowly.

### Frame C04-F02 — Ask for meaning or wording

**Meaning after pointing to or repeating an item**

> الكلمة، واش معناها؟  
> `l-kelma, waash maʿna-ha?`  
> The word—what does it mean?

In ordinary interaction, you can also point to the word or repeat it and ask simply:

> واش معناها؟  
> `waash maʿna-ha?`  
> What does it mean?

**Wording**

> كيفاش نقولو “[X]” بالدارجة؟  
> `kiifash ngulu “[X]” b-ddarža?`  
> How do we say “[X]” in Darja?

`[X]` may be an English or French word, a gesture, or an idea both speakers can identify. Keep the unknown expression in its original language; do not present it as Darja.

### Frame C04-F03 — Correct and reformulate

> لا، ماشي [X]. قصدي [Y].  
> `laa, maashi [X]. qṣdi [Y].`  
> No, not [X]. I mean [Y].

Keep `[X]` short. Repeating someone’s entire mistaken statement can sound argumentative.

**Examples**

1. لا، ماشي سامي. قصدي سام.  
   `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`  
   No, not Sami. I mean Sam.

2. `[X]، يعني [Y].`  
   `[X], yaʿni [Y].`  
   “[X], meaning [Y].”

**Listening-only expansion**

> بشوية، يعني ما تزربش.  
> `b-shwiya, yaʿni ma tezreb-sh.`  
> More slowly—that is, don’t rush.

`ma tezreb-sh` is not productive in Section 1.

**Need:** After correcting the name, the organiser checks which language will work. The French turn is a plausible accommodation attempt for this speaker profile, not something every Algerian would say.

### Listening support

- `ṣaḥḥ?`, `tehder b-l-fransawiyya?` and the language labels recycle Chapter 3 material.
- `bṣaḥ` and turn-final `ṣaḥḥa` are listening support here, not new Chapter 4 targets.
- *Tu parles français ?* is active French code-switching (`FR-CS`), not an integrated Darja loan.
- Human bilingual recording is required because the transition into and out of French is part of the lesson.

## 8. Pronunciation and rhythm

### Keep the negative frame together

`ma` and `-sh` frame the verb:

- careful: `ma fhemt-sh`
- connected group: `ma_fhemt-sh`
- full response: `smaḥli / ma_fhemt-sh //`

Place the main beat on `fhemt`:

> `smaḥli / ma_**FHEMT**-sh // ʿaawed_b-**SHWI**ya //`

### The `fh` cluster

Begin `fhemt` with `f`, release directly into `h`, then continue to `emt`. Avoid a strong inserted vowel such as *fa-hemt*. A very brief transition may occur naturally in reviewed recordings, but do not build a full extra syllable.

### `ʿaawed`

- The word begins with `ʿ`; a beginner approximation is acceptable initially, but the consonant remains in course spelling.
- Hold the first `aa` slightly: `ʿaa-wed`.
- Do not turn the word into English *owed*.

### Friendly correction rhythm

> `laa / maashi **SAA**mi // qṣdi **SAM** //`

Let the pitch settle on the correct item. High volume and a sharp final rise can make the correction sound confrontational.

### Shadowing groups

1. `smaḥli / ma_fhemt-sh //`
2. `ʿaawed_b-shwiya / men_faḍlek //`
3. `laa / maashi Saami // qṣdi Sam //`
4. `smaḥli / waash_maʿna-ha? //`

## 9. Pragmatics

### Identify the kind of problem

A specific repair request helps your partner respond:

- repeat: `ʿaawed`;
- reduce speed: `ʿaawed b-shwiya`;
- explain: `waash maʿna-ha?`;
- supply Darja wording: `kiifash ngulu… b-ddarža?`.

If the problem is sound rather than meaning, you may hear `ma smeʿt-sh mliiḥ`—“I didn’t hear properly.” Keep it recognition-only for now.

### Directness and warmth

Bare `ʿaawed` can be efficient among supportive peers. With a stranger, employee, elder or someone already making an effort, add a softener or use warm intonation:

- `smaḥli, ʿaawed.`
- `ʿaawed, men faḍlek.`
- `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh.`

The precise social distribution of `men faḍlek` requires native pragmatic review.

### Correct the detail, not the person

Prefer:

> `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`

The short correction resolves the practical problem without assigning blame.

### Translation is one resource, not a requirement

If both people share another language, brief translation may help. Otherwise, request slower speech, a simpler Darja explanation, a gesture or an example. Do not assume French will help every Algerian speaker—or every learner.

## 10. You may hear…

### An alternative non-understanding frame

> ما رانيش فاهم / فاهمة  
> `ma raanish fahem / faahma`

This is roughly “I’m not following,” with masculine `fahem` and feminine `faahma`. Recognise it, but continue producing `ma fhemt-sh`. Its central frequency and pragmatic contrast remain `NV-OPEN`.

### A French repair turn

Some bilingual speakers in some settings may use French *Pardon ?* or another brief French clarification turn. Treat it as active code-switching, not compulsory Darja. Arabic–French switching is documented in spoken and online corpora, but its density and function vary by speaker and situation ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf)).

### Forms built on `ʿqel`

In some communities and expressions, `bel-ʿqel` or `b-ʿaqlek` may ask someone to calm down, take care, lower their voice or go slowly. This is a vocabulary and pragmatic difference, not merely another pronunciation of `b-shwiya`. Continue producing `ʿaawed b-shwiya` for slower repetition. The cited evidence is local to Fadj M’Zala/Ferdjioua and does not establish nationwide usage.

## 11. Rooted context — Using the whole repertoire

**Claim classification:** `DOC-POLICY`, `EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`.

Algerian language practice takes place in a multilingual landscape involving Darja, multiple Tamazight varieties, formal Arabic, French and—depending on speaker and setting—other languages. Scholarship connects present practices to education, access, identity, colonial and postcolonial policy and globalisation, but does not support reducing every individual language choice to one motive ([Rouabah 2022, 21–40](https://www.thebritishacademy.ac.uk/publishing/journal-british-academy/10s4/multilingualism-in-algeria/)).

French has a documented historical and institutional relationship to colonisation and later language policy. A contemporary Arabic–French switch may nevertheless serve an immediate purpose such as naming a familiar concept, quoting, accommodating, reformulating or repairing a breakdown. Corpora establish that switching occurs; they do not show that everyone switches in the same way or for the same reason ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf)).

**Course interpretation:** choosing a resource that restores shared understanding is practical interactional intelligence. Multilingual practice should not automatically be labelled deficiency, but learners should not assume that French is necessary for authentic Algerian speech ([Bouchiba Ghlamallah 2015](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/article/6350)).

## 12. Practice ladder

### A. Notice the two parts

Underline the problem signal and circle the requested solution.

1. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed.`
2. `ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
3. `smaḥli, waash maʿna-ha?`

### B. Choose the useful response

1. The sentence was too fast.  
   a. `qṣdi Sam.`  
   b. `ʿaawed b-shwiya.`  
   c. `kiifash ngulu Sam?`

2. You understand everything except one word.  
   a. `l-kelma, waash maʿna-ha?`  
   b. `maashi.`  
   c. `laa laa.`

3. Someone calls you Sami, but your name is Sam.  
   a. `yaʿni Saami.`  
   b. `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`  
   c. `ʿaawed Saami.`

**Answers:** 1b, 2a, 3b.

### C. Substitution

Start with:

> `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed.`

Now add:

1. “please”;
2. “more slowly”;
3. both.

**Possible responses**

1. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed, men faḍlek.`
2. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
3. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya, men faḍlek.`

Then replace the names in `laa, maashi X. qṣdi Y`:

- Lina → Lena
- Adam → Ed
- Sara → the pronunciation of Sarah intended by the learner

The names are task slots, not Darja vocabulary.

### D. Cued retrieval

Cover the key and speak from the English.

1. Sorry, I didn’t understand.
2. Repeat more slowly, please.
3. What does it mean?
4. How do we say “repeat” in Darja?
5. No, not Sami. I mean Sam.
6. Meaning…

**Key**

1. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh.`
2. `ʿaawed b-shwiya, men faḍlek.`
3. `waash maʿna-ha?`
4. `kiifash ngulu “repeat” b-ddarža?`
5. `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`
6. `yaʿni…`

### E. Three-second responses

| Cue | Response goal |
|---|---|
| A fast, unclear sentence | signal non-understanding and request slower repetition |
| An unknown word | ask its meaning |
| “Your name is Sami?” | correct it to Sam |
| A French sentence you do not understand | ask what it means without criticising the switch |
| You need the Darja word for an idea | ask how to say it in Darja |

### F. Mixed retrieval

Using earlier greetings, identity and language material:

1. greet the organiser;
2. give your name;
3. fail to understand one question;
4. request repetition;
5. correct one mistaken detail;
6. say which language you prefer;
7. close the exchange.

### G. Repair round

A partner creates one natural breakdown by:

- saying one known line too quickly;
- mishearing one name or place;
- using one clearly labelled French switch;
- pointing to a word and asking what it means.

Repair the problem and then finish the original task. A repair that abandons the task is incomplete.

## 19. Native-review questions

1. Is `ma fhemt-sh` the preferred broad beginner form for this central baseline? When would `ma raanish fahem/faahma` be more natural?
2. Confirm the course pronunciation and spelling `ʿaawed`; compare it with `ʿawed` in careful and ordinary speech.
3. Is `ʿaawed b-shwiya` natural for “repeat more slowly,” or should the productive frame use another wording?

5. Is `waash maʿna-ha?` sufficient after a French sentence or pointed word?

10. Are `smek Saami?`, bare `smaḥli?` and `Saami, ṣaḥḥ?` plausible for the relationship shown?
11. Is *Tu parles français ?* a plausible accommodation attempt for the proposed bilingual speaker without another lead-in? Mark `NV-CS`.
12. Is `tehder b-l-fransawiyya?` the preferred explanation, and is `shwiya, bṣaḥ b-ddarža men faḍlek` natural in this order?
13. Is final `ṣaḥḥa` suitable here? If not, replace it with already productive closing material.
14. Should `bel-ʿqel/b-ʿaqlek` remain a tightly bounded recognition note or be removed from this repair context?
15. Review all script spacing, the rhythm group `ma_fhemt-sh`, the `fh` cluster and every transition into or out of French.
16. Confirm that no recording exaggerates a central-Algiers identity or performs French as a social stereotype.

# Section 1 synthesis — Hear, enter and keep going

Across this section, you built one connected interaction engine:

1. **hear and identify:** recognise the rhythm, confirm who is speaking and say you are present;
2. **greet and respond:** open warmly, check wellbeing, welcome someone and close;
3. **give a compact profile:** name, origin, residence and language ability;
4. **repair the exchange:** signal a problem, request help, ask for meaning and correct a detail.

The goal is not a perfect uninterrupted performance. It is a short conversation that survives ordinary misunderstandings.

**Organiser:** السلام عليكم، مرحبا.  
`s-slaam ʿlikom, marḥba.`

**Visitor:** وعليكم السلام.  
`w-ʿlikom s-slaam.`  
Hello.

**Organiser:** اسمك لينا؟  
`smek Liina?`  
Is your name Lina?

**Visitor:** لا، ماشي لينا. قصدي لينا.  
`laa, maashi Liina. qṣdi Lena.`  
No, not Lina. I mean Lena.

**Organiser:** آه، لينا. من وين نتي؟  
`aah, Lena. men wiin nti?`  
Oh, Lena. Where are you from?

**Visitor:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية، من فضلك.  
`smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya, men faḍlek.`  
Sorry, I didn’t understand. Say it again more slowly, please.

**Organiser:** من وين نتي؟  
`men wiin nti?`  
Where are you from?

**Visitor:** أنا من لندن، نسكن في العاصمة. نفهم الدارجة شوية.  
`ana men London, neskon fi l-ʿaasma. nefhem d-darža shwiya.`  
I’m from London, I live in Algiers. I understand a little Darja.

**Organiser:** بالدارجة؟  
`b-ddarža?`  
In Darja?

**Visitor:** إييه، بالدارجة من فضلك.  
`iih, b-ddarža men faḍlek.`  
Yes, in Darja, please.

**Organiser:** صحا، مرحبا بيك.  
`ṣaḥḥa, marḥba bik.`  
Okay, welcome.

## Section exit task — Join the language exchange

### Communicative outcome

Complete a two-minute first meeting and leave with a correctly written partner card.

### Partner card

Your partner must finish with:

| Information | Required result |
|---|---|
| Name | correctly recorded |
| Origin | correctly recorded |
| Current residence | correctly recorded |
| Darja ability | none/a little/more, using known material |
| Preferred conversation language | correctly recorded |

### Required stages

1. Exchange an appropriate greeting.
2. Ask and answer about wellbeing.
3. Give or request a name.
4. Give origin and current residence.
5. State language ability or preference.
6. Repair one fast question.
7. Correct one deliberately misrecorded detail.
8. Close the exchange.

### Hidden information gap

Each learner privately chooses or receives:

- a name;
- a place of origin;
- a current residence;
- one known language-strength description;
- a preferred conversation language.

Do not show the card. The organiser records answers from speech alone.

### Forced breakdowns

The organiser must:

- say one familiar question too quickly;
- repeat one name or place incorrectly.

The participant must use:

- `ma fhemt-sh`;
- `ʿaawed b-shwiya`;
- `maashi X, qṣdi Y`;
- one greeting and one closing from Chapter 2.

Use `waash maʿna-ha?` or `kiifash ngulu… b-ddarža?` if another genuine gap occurs.

### Success conditions

You succeed if:

- the final partner card is accurate;
- both breakdowns are repaired;
- the conversation returns to its original task after each repair;
- you speak from prompts rather than reading a complete script.

### Self-study adaptation

1. Create a profile card with five details.
2. Record the organiser’s questions, including one fast question and one mistaken confirmation.
3. Wait at least fifteen minutes.
4. Play the recording and answer without reading a script.
5. Repair the fast question before replaying it.
6. Correct the mistaken detail aloud.
7. Record a second version at a comfortable natural pace.
8. Compare outcomes, not accent perfection: Was every final detail clear and correct?

## Exit self-check

Mark each statement **now**, **with support** or **not yet**.

- I can recognise and produce the main Section 1 greetings.
- I can give my name, origin, residence and limited language ability.
- I can distinguish Darja from an active French switch in the lesson transcripts.
- I can say I did not understand without blaming the speaker.
- I can request repetition and reduced speed.
- I can ask what something means or how to say it in Darja.

## Section 1 takeaway

You do not need to understand everything before entering a conversation. A greeting, a compact identity frame and a reliable repair sequence are enough to begin:

> `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`

That sentence does more than report a problem: it gives your partner a clear way to help, so the conversation can continue.
