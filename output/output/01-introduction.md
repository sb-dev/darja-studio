# Introduction — How to use *Speak Algerian Arabic*

## What this course is for

This course helps you build independent conversations in Algerian Darja: asking for what you need, responding naturally, telling short stories, making plans and repairing misunderstandings.

You will learn through a **sentence engine**, not through isolated word lists. Each chapter starts with a realistic purpose and gives you a small set of reusable frames. For example, one frame may let you say:

- I want **X**.
- I do not want **X**.
- Do you want **X**?
- I wanted **X**, but…

You then replace **X**, change the person or time, and use the frame in a genuine exchange. New language repeatedly returns in dialogues, listening tasks, reviews and section challenges.

The goal is not to memorise every line. It is to gain control of patterns that let you make new lines of your own.

## Productive and recognition language

The course distinguishes two learning targets:

- **[P] Productive:** learn this well enough to say it yourself. Retrieve it without looking and reuse it in new combinations.
- **[R] Recognition:** learn to understand it when you hear or read it. You are not expected to adopt it as your own form.

Across the course, the controlled vocabulary ledger contains approximately **380 productive items** and **150 recognition items**. A chapter may practise more words than it officially adds because earlier productive language keeps returning.

Recognition items often include regional alternatives, less suitable forms for the course baseline, or language that is useful to understand before you need to produce it.

## Which Algerian Darja?

There is no claim here that one Algerian way of speaking is the only correct one. For consistency, the main productive model is a **broadly useful central urban baseline associated mainly with Algiers**.

You may also encounter bounded notes such as:

- **[REG-W]** You may hear this in western Algeria.
- **[REG-E]** You may hear this in eastern Algeria.
- **[REG-S]** You may hear this in Saharan contexts or particular southern communities.
- **[AMZ]** Amazigh-origin, Amazigh-influenced or contact-related usage, included only where the evidence supports that description.
- **[SOC] / [GEN]** Usage associated with a particular social setting or generation.

These notes build listening flexibility. They do not imply that everyone in a large region speaks alike. Unless a chapter explicitly promotes a regional form to productive status, treat it as recognition language.

## Darja, Arabic and French labels

The course keeps different kinds of language visible:

- **[DZ]** Ordinary Darja in the course baseline.
- **[MSA]** Modern Standard Arabic, included for recognition or a specific formal purpose.
- **[FR→DZ]** A French-derived word functioning as an integrated part of Darja in the example.
- **[FR-SW]** Active switching into French rather than an integrated loanword.
- **[AMZ]** Amazigh-origin or contact-influenced material where supported.

A French-looking word is therefore not automatically labelled a code-switch. Pay attention to how the whole utterance is framed, who is speaking and what the situation requires. The dialogues sometimes include realistic switching, but you will also learn ways to keep an interaction moving when you do not share the same French repertoire.

## Arabic script and course transliteration

Every important utterance appears in three forms:

1. **Arabic script**
2. **Pedagogical transliteration**
3. **Natural English meaning**

You do **not** need to master Arabic script before speaking. Begin with the audio and transliteration, then use the script as a second route into the same phrase. The course maintains a consistent teaching spelling so that script study supports conversation rather than delaying it.

The transliteration is a pronunciation aid, not informal Arabizi. It does not replace sounds with numerals. Its main conventions include:

| Symbol | Course function |
|---|---|
| `aa`, `ii`, `uu` | Long vowels |
| `e` | A course vowel symbol; learn its exact quality from the recording |
| `ḥ` | The consonant represented by Arabic **ح** |
| `ʿ` | The consonant represented by Arabic **ع** |
| `sh`, `zh` | The sounds commonly heard in English *ship* and French *journal* |
| `q`, `g` | Written according to the selected spoken form; variation is flagged where relevant |
| `ṣ`, `ḍ`, `ṭ`, `ẓ` | Emphatic consonants |

Do not let unfamiliar symbols stop you. Listen first, imitate the whole rhythmic group, and return to individual sounds afterward. Notes on linking, reduction and word grouping show how the natural recording may differ from a careful word-by-word reading.

## How to use the audio

Important language is normally prepared in four listening modes:

1. **Careful phrase:** clear enough to identify the words and sounds.
2. **Natural phrase:** the same language with ordinary conversational rhythm.
3. **Shadowing groups:** short rhythmic pieces with pauses for imitation.
4. **Response or contrast:** a prompt requiring you to answer, correct, choose or disagree.

“Careful” does not mean Modern Standard Arabic, and “natural” does not mean careless. They are two performances of the same communicative language.

Some mechanical review clips may be prototyped with text-to-speech. Anchor dialogues, socially delicate interaction, regional comparison, humour, marked code-switching and sensitive historical material are designated for human recording. The course never asks generic TTS to imitate an unsupported regional accent.

### A simple shadowing cycle

For one short utterance:

1. Listen without reading.
2. Listen again while following the script or transliteration.
3. Repeat during the pause.
4. Shadow the speaker with a slight delay.
5. Say the line alone with the same intention.
6. Change one element and say a new line.

Aim for the speaker’s grouping, stress and emotional purpose—not just the individual consonants.

## Retrieval matters more than rereading

Recognition can feel like mastery when the answer is still visible. Each chapter therefore asks you to **retrieve** language:

- cover the Darja and speak from the English cue;
- hear a prompt and answer before the model;
- rebuild a frame with a different person, object, place or time;
- record yourself completing a one-minute task;
- return later without reviewing first.

Difficulty retrieving is useful information. Check the answer, say it correctly, and test it again after a delay. Keep difficult phrases in meaningful chunks rather than splitting everything into single words.

## A practical study routine

A chapter can be studied in several short sessions. A suggested routine is **25–35 minutes**, adjusted to your schedule.

### First pass: understand and imitate

1. Read the chapter outcome and situation.
2. Preview the productive items; scan recognition notes without trying to memorise them.
3. Listen to the accessible dialogue once without reading.
4. Listen again with Arabic script, transliteration and English.
5. Shadow three to five useful lines.

### Second pass: operate the sentence engine

1. Practise the main frames aloud.
2. Make substitutions rather than repeating only the printed examples.
3. Change positive statements into negatives or questions when instructed.
4. Complete the listening task before checking the transcript.
5. Perform the information-gap or personalised speaking task.

### Third pass: retrieve and interact

1. Hide the answers.
2. Respond to audio prompts within the pause.
3. Retell or reconstruct the chapter situation in your own words.
4. Record one attempt, listen back and repeat once.
5. Finish with the chapter’s repair language: ask for repetition, clarification or confirmation instead of abandoning the exchange.

Revisit the hardest items after a short gap and again later in the week. At the end of each section, use the exit task to combine older and newer language without following a script.

## Using the book, web materials and audio together

- **Book or printable text:** annotate frames, cover answers and mark items you cannot yet retrieve.
- **Web version:** use reveal-and-hide activities, shuffled prompts and linked audio where provided.
- **Audio-only review:** practise while walking or doing a simple task, but return later to an activity that requires a real response.
- **Personal recording:** compare communicative clarity, rhythm and response time—not whether your voice exactly matches the model.

If you have only ten minutes, listen to one dialogue, shadow two lines and answer three prompts. Regular active recall is more useful than waiting for a perfect long session.

## Native-review flags

Some forms require further checking by speakers with the relevant regional and social experience. These are marked:

- **`NV-OPEN`** — native-speaker validation is still open.

Possible review points include pronunciation, vowel choice, `q/g` realisation, question forms, negation with attached pronouns, future or `raa-` constructions, French switching, spelling, politeness and ordinary-speed reductions.

Treat an `NV-OPEN` form as provisional. Do not generalise it to all Algerian speakers, and do not prefer it over a reviewed productive form unless the task specifically asks you to compare them.

## Culture and history: context, not decoration

Cultural material is included to make communication more grounded: everyday routines, hospitality, food, neighbourhood life, work, music, humour, migration, creativity and contemporary ambitions. Historical material appears only when it has a relevant connection to language, memory, education, place, family or community practice, solidarity, resistance or migration.

The course distinguishes among:

- a **documented event or policy**;
- a person’s or family’s **oral memory**;
- a wider **collective narrative**;
- the course writer’s **editorial interpretation**.

A personal memory is not presented as the experience of an entire country. Sensitive material is sourced cautiously and prioritised for human rather than synthetic recording. You are invited to listen respectfully, notice whose perspective is represented and avoid turning cultural information into fixed rules about Algerian people.

## Your working principle

For each chapter, ask:

> **What can I now do with another person that I could not do before?**

Learn a small number of productive forms deeply, understand a wider range of recognition forms, and practise recovering when conversation becomes difficult. That combination—not perfect recitation—is the route toward independent conversation.
