# Chapter 1 — What Algerian Arabic sounds like

> **Publication warning:** Every productive form in this first draft remains `NV-OPEN`. The anchor dialogues require two independent central-urban/Algiers reviews before recording or publication.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- say who you are;
- check whether you have reached the right person;
- say that you are here;
- say that you did not understand;
- ask someone to repeat more slowly;
- recognise the difference between careful pronunciation and ordinary connected speech.

### Situation

You have arrived at a busy meeting point for a language exchange. You call the contact number you were given. Traffic and other voices make the first answer hard to catch, so you confirm the person’s name and ask for a slower repetition.

The goal is **not** to sound perfect. The goal is to remain in the conversation.

---

## 2. Before you speak

Listen to the ordinary-speed dialogue once without reading.

1. How many separate words do you think you hear in `ما فهمتش`?
2. Which speaker has difficulty hearing?
3. Does the conversation succeed, or does someone give up?

Do not try to identify every consonant yet.

## 5. The sentence engine

### Frame C01-F01 — Identify yourself

| Field | Specification |
|---|---|
| Function | Give your identity. |
| Arabic skeleton | `أنا + [الاسم]` |
| Transliteration | `ana + [name]` |
| Natural English | “I’m [name].” / “My name is [name].” |
| Fixed material | `ana` |
| Slot | A personal name or immediately relevant identity label. Only names are practised here. |
| Grammar | Darja does not need a spoken present-tense equivalent of English *am* in this identity frame. |
| Negation | Not introduced for this frame. |
| Pragmatics | Neutral self-identification; usually accompanied by suitable eye contact or phone-call context. |
| Validation | `NV-OPEN` |

**Example 1**

- **Arabic:** أنا نورا.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Nura.`
- **English:** I’m Noura.

**Example 2**

- **Arabic:** أنا سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m Samir.

### Frame C01-F02 — Check a person’s identity

| Field | Specification |
|---|---|
| Function | Check whether you have reached the expected person. |
| Arabic skeleton | `نتا / نتي + [الاسم]؟` |
| Transliteration | `nta / nti + [name]?` |
| Natural English | “Are you [name]?” |
| Fixed material | `nta` to one male addressee; `nti` to one female addressee in the provisional baseline. |
| Slot | A personal name. |
| Grammar | The question is signalled here by rising or checking intonation; no question particle is required in this taught frame. |
| Response | `iih` for yes; `la` followed by `ana + [name]` for correction. |

**Example 1**

- **Arabic:** نتا سمير؟
- **Transliteration:** `nta Smiir?`
- **English:** Are you Samir?

**Example 2**

- **Arabic:** نتي نورا؟
- **Transliteration:** `nti Nura?`
- **English:** Are you Noura?

**Correction sequence**

- **Arabic:** لا. أنا لينا.
- **Transliteration:** `la. ana Liina.`
- **English:** No. I’m Lina.

## 6. Accessible dialogue

### Purpose

Noura calls the meeting-point contact. She confirms that she has reached Samir, reports that she is there, and repairs one unclear turn.

### Relationship and setting

- two adults meeting for the first time;
- phone call at a noisy public entrance;
- cooperative, neutral-warm tone;
- no French switch is scripted.

### Dialogue

**1. نورا**

- **Arabic:** سلام. نتا سمير؟
- **Transliteration:** `salaam. nta Smiir?`
- **English:** Hi. Are you Samir?

**2. سمير**

- **Arabic:** إيه. نتي نورا؟
- **Transliteration:** `iih. nti Nura?`
- **English:** Yes. Are you Noura?

**3. نورا**

- **Arabic:** إيه. أنا نورا. راني هنا.
- **Transliteration:** `iih. ana Nura. raani hna.`
- **English:** Yes. I’m Noura. I’m here.

**4. سمير**

- **Arabic:** أنا سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m Samir.

**5. نورا**

- **Arabic:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿawed b-shwiya.`
- **English:** Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat more slowly.

**6. سمير**

- **Arabic:** أنا سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m Samir.

**7. نورا**

- **Arabic:** إيه.
- **Transliteration:** `iih.`
- **English:** Yes; right.

### Result

Both speakers confirm the correct contact. Noura does not pretend to understand: she repairs the problem and the call continues.

## 7. Understand the exchange

Answer from meaning, not from individual word recognition.

1. Who makes the call?
2. What does Noura need to confirm?
3. Which line reports arrival at the meeting point?
4. What goes wrong after Samir identifies himself?
5. Does Noura ask Samir to translate, repeat or leave a message?
6. What is the practical result of the call?

**Suggested answers:** Noura calls; she confirms Samir’s identity; line 3 reports that she is there; she does not catch Samir’s name; she asks for repetition at a slower pace; they confirm the correct contact.

### Pronunciation track only — pending native review

These markings are rehearsal proposals, not claims about a universal Algerian pronunciation.

```text
saLAAM / nta smiir? //
IIH / nti NUra? //
IIH / RAAni_hna //
smaḥ-li / ma_FHEMT-sh // ʿawed_b-SHWIya //
```

Possible ordinary-speed effects to test in recording:

- `raani hna` forms one close rhythm group rather than two isolated dictionary words;
- `ma fhemt-sh` is heard as one compact repair unit;
- the weak vowel in `fhemt` is short and central rather than a full English-style vowel;
- `smaḥ-li` and `hna` begin with consonant sequences that English speakers may try to break apart;
- names carry the main identifying beat.

All reductions and beat placements above require `NV-AUDIO` confirmation.

## 9. What you are hearing

### 9.1 Darja is not defective MSA

Algerian Darja is the ordinary spoken language of interaction for many Algerians. It differs from Modern Standard Arabic across sound patterns, morphology, vocabulary and syntax; it is therefore misleading to treat it as MSA pronounced carelessly. Published Algerian-language resources also emphasise internal regional variation and the absence of one universally settled everyday spelling system ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208/), pp. 69–75; [Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf), pp. 384–390). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf))

`EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`: In this course, **oral and adaptive** means that Darja is shaped for rapid interaction, local relationships and multilingual life. It does not mean careless, broken or grammatically empty.

### 9.2 Fewer audible vowels does not mean “no vowels”

Maghrebi speech commonly permits compact consonant sequences and reduced short vowels. Algerian orthographic work also documents vowel shortening and differences between written and surface forms ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf), pp. 74–75). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf))

Compare:

- **Arabic:** ما فهمتش.
- **Transliteration:** `ma fhemt-sh.`
- **English:** I didn’t understand.

Do **not** expand this into an imagined formal form such as *ma fa-him-tu-sh*. Keep the centre compact:

```text
ma | fhemt | sh
     ^ main beat
```

The `e` in `fhemt` is a short reduced teaching vowel. Its exact quality must follow reviewed audio.

### 9.3 Words connect into rhythm groups

In ordinary speech, speakers organise language around meaning and emphasis, not around spaces on a page.

- **Careful:** `raani / hna`
- **Natural target:** `raani_hna`
- **English:** I’m here.

- **Careful:** `ma / fhemt / sh`
- **Natural target:** `ma_fhemt-sh`
- **English:** I didn’t understand.

The underscore is a teaching mark for close rhythm. It is not punctuation and does not mean that the Arabic words must be respelled.

### 9.4 Careful speech and ordinary speech are both real

Careful speech is not fake. A cooperative speaker may separate a phrase after you request repetition. Ordinary speech then reconnects it.

**First attempt**

- **Arabic:** أنا سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m Samir.

**Repair request**

- **Arabic:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿawed b-shwiya.`
- **English:** Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat more slowly.

**Careful response**

- **Arabic:** أنا / سمير.
- **Transliteration:** `ana / Smiir.`
- **English:** I’m / Samir.

Your aim is to understand both versions, not to force every speaker to remain at classroom speed.

## 10. Essential consonants: a first map

You do not need to master the entire sound system today. Preserve the symbol and build the sound gradually.

| Symbol | Arabic | Beginner direction | Chapter example | Common risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `ḥ` | ح | Strong, voiceless breath made lower in the throat than English `h`. Do not add voice. | `smaḥ-li` — سمحلي — excuse me/sorry | Replacing it completely with a light English `h`. |
| `ʿ` | ع | A brief narrowing in the throat. Begin gently; do not turn it into `g`, `r` or a long vowel. | `ʿawed` — عاود — repeat | Deleting the consonant and beginning only with a vowel. |
| `h` | ه | Ordinary breathy `h`. | `hna` — هنا — here | Adding a large vowel before `hn-`. |
| `q` | ق | A stop made farther back than `k`. Item-specific pronunciation varies. | `qahwa` — قهوة — coffee `[R]` | Converting every ق in Algeria by one automatic rule. |
| `kh` | خ | Voiceless friction like Scottish *loch* or German *Bach*. | No productive word yet. | Pronouncing `k` + `h` as two separate sounds. |
| `gh` | غ | Voiced partner of `kh`. | No productive word yet. | Replacing it automatically with English hard `g`. |
| `zh` | often ج when reviewed | Sound in English *measure*. The course uses it only where central-baseline audio supports it. | No productive word yet. | Assuming every ج has one Algerian pronunciation. |
| `ṭ ḍ ṣ ẓ` | ط ض ص ظ | Backed or “emphatic” consonants that can colour nearby vowels. | No productive word yet. | Treating the difference as a spelling detail only. |
| doubled consonant | often shown with ّ | Hold the closure or friction longer; do not insert a vowel. | No productive contrast yet. | Saying two separate syllables instead of one longer consonant. |

Gemination is represented in the course by doubled consonants in transliteration and, where useful, shadda in Arabic script. Algerian phonological descriptions treat a written shadda as consonant length rather than as an extra syllable ([Harrat et al. 2016](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf), pp. 389–390). ([thesai.org](https://thesai.org/Downloads/Volume7No3/Paper_53-An_Algerian_dialect_Study_and_Resources.pdf))

### Micro-practice: clusters without extra syllables

Keep each item on one beat.

1. **Arabic:** هنا
   - **Transliteration:** `hna`
   - **English:** here

2. **Arabic:** سمحلي
   - **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li`
   - **English:** excuse me; sorry

3. **Arabic:** فهمت
   - **Transliteration:** `fhemt`
   - **English:** I understood — used here only inside the taught negative chunk

Try this progression:

```text
h...na → hna
s...maḥ-li → smaḥ-li
f...hemt → fhemt
```

Do not race. Compact does not mean tense.

## 11. Arabic script, course transliteration and Arabizi

### 11.1 Three systems with different jobs

| System | Example | Job |
|---|---|---|
| Pedagogical Arabic script | ما فهمتش | Supports Arabic literacy and recognition while following the course’s stable editorial conventions. |
| Course transliteration | `ma fhemt-sh` | Shows the reviewed teaching pronunciation, morpheme boundaries and consonant distinctions. |
| Informal Arabizi | attested spellings include forms such as `mafhemtch` | Represents variable user-created digital writing. It is recognition literacy, not the course pronunciation key. |

Algerian corpora document Arabic script, Latin script and mixed writing, with substantial orthographic variability rather than one fixed public Arabizi standard ([Touileb & Barnes 2021](https://aclanthology.org/2021.findings-acl.324/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf)). ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.07400?utm_source=openai))

### 11.2 How to read the Arabic line

Arabic is written from right to left. Short vowels are normally not written. The course therefore gives you audio and transliteration rather than expecting the Arabic spelling alone to reveal every vowel.

**Example**

- **Arabic:** راني هنا.
- **Transliteration:** `raani hna.`
- **English:** I’m here.

The Arabic spelling does not separately mark every detail shown by `aa` or by the teaching vowel in `hna`.

### 11.3 How to read course transliteration

- `aa`, `ii`, `uu` show longer vowels.
- `e` is a short central or reduced teaching vowel.
- `ḥ` and `ʿ` preserve consonants absent from ordinary English spelling.
- a hyphen reveals a useful teaching boundary: `smaḥ-li`, `ma …-sh`.
- an underscore appears only in pronunciation work and means “keep this close”: `raani_hna`.

### 11.4 Arabizi recognition preview

Numerals can represent Arabic consonants that lack an obvious Latin equivalent. In many Arabizi traditions, including material used in Algerian computational work, `3` may represent ع and `7` may represent ح; mappings and spellings still vary by writer and context ([Guellil et al. 2018](https://aclanthology.org/2018.jeptalnrecital-court.35/); [Touileb & Barnes 2021](https://aclanthology.org/2021.findings-acl.324/)). ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.03437?utm_source=openai))

| Arabizi sign `[R]` | Arabic consonant | Course transliteration | Example component |
|---|---|---|---|
| `3` | ع | `ʿ` | `ʿawed` — عاود — repeat |
| `7` | ح | `ḥ` | `smaḥ-li` — سمحلي — excuse me/sorry |

Do not mechanically rewrite the course as `3` and `7`. Real Arabizi spellings must be encountered or elicited, not invented by conversion.

## 12. Pragmatics

### Repair is participation, not failure

A beginner who says nothing after missing a phrase may accidentally signal agreement or comprehension. A short repair sequence keeps both people coordinated.

**Recommended sequence**

- **Arabic:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥ-li, ma fhemt-sh. ʿawed b-shwiya.`
- **English:** Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat more slowly.

### Tone and relationship

- Use a calm or slightly apologetic tone with a stranger.
- `smaḥ-li` helps soften the direct imperative `ʿawed`.
- A bare `ʿawed!` may sound abrupt depending on tone, relationship and locality; it is not the beginner default.
- `la` is not inherently rude, but a sharp, loud or prolonged delivery can be confrontational.
- `iih` can confirm identity, but its vowel and pragmatic contrast with other Algerian acknowledgement forms require native review.

### Do not imitate speed by swallowing everything

Natural speech preserves contrasts even when vowels are short. If your attempt becomes unintelligible, return to the careful phrase, then reconnect it gradually.

## 13. You may hear…

### Item-specific pronunciation of ق

**Recognition only. Do not generalise this as an accent conversion rule.**

The course baseline writes and provisionally transliterates the carrier word as:

- **Arabic:** قهوة
- **Transliteration:** `qahwa`
- **English:** coffee

Published work describes reflexes of historical ق including `q`, `g`, a glottal stop and `k` across Algerian locations and lexical items. The same orthographic word may consequently be heard with different initial consonants. The cited resource reports `q` in some urban speech including Algiers, `g` in locations including Annaba and Sétif and in some Bedouin-associated varieties, a glottal realisation in Tlemcen, and `k` in specified northern localities; these broad descriptions must not replace local, item-by-item review ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf), pp. 71, 75–76). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208.pdf))

| Status | Possible heard form | Scope statement |
|---|---|---|
| Provisional course baseline | `qahwa` | Requires central-urban review before productive use. |
| Recognition | `gahwa` | You may hear it from speakers and in communities using `g` in this lexical item. |
| Recognition | `’ahwa` | You may hear a glottal onset in some local speech, including forms reported for Tlemcen. |
| Recognition | `kahwa` | You may hear `k` in some specified northern varieties. |

**Learner action:** recognise that the word may be related; do not “translate” every `q` into `g`, `’` or `k`.

No generic TTS regional-comparison clip is authorised. Any future comparison requires appropriately profiled human speakers and regional validation.

## 14. Rooted context: an oral language can also be written

**Claim classes:** `DOC-POLICY`, `EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`.

Darja has long been centred on oral interaction, while formal Arabic has occupied many formal written domains. That division does not make everyday speech structurally deficient. Algerian Arabic research describes differences from MSA at every major linguistic level and has proposed specialised orthographies precisely because existing everyday writing is variable rather than absent ([Saadane & Habash 2015](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208/), pp. 69–75). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/W15-3208/?utm_source=openai))

Digital communication has made Algerian speech more visible in Arabic script, Latin script and mixed forms. Corpora assembled from online comments document this written adaptability, including multilingual material and inconsistent spelling ([Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf); [Touileb & Barnes 2021](https://aclanthology.org/2021.findings-acl.324/)). ([cis.upenn.edu](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf?utm_source=openai))

`EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`: The course therefore treats Arabic script, transliteration and Arabizi as tools with different purposes. None is used to judge whether someone’s spoken Darja is “pure.”

Colonial history is not expanded here because this chapter does not yet provide enough language to examine language hierarchy and French–Arabic contact responsibly. Later chapters should connect those topics to documented education, administration, migration and present multilingual practice rather than attaching colonial history decoratively to pronunciation.

## 20. Native-review questions

### Productive language

1. In a contemporary Algiers/periphery baseline, is `إيه — iih` the best beginner production choice for an uncomplicated “yes”? Record its natural vowel quality and compare it with `آه — aah` or other common acknowledgements without promoting alternatives automatically.
2. Is `سمحلي — smaḥ-li` the preferred course spelling and pronunciation, or would reviewers favour another stable Arabic spelling such as `سماحلي` for the intended spoken form?
3. Does `سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.` sound suitably polite between unfamiliar adults? If marked, revise the request rather than merely changing the English gloss.
4. Is `بشوية — b-shwiya` natural for “more slowly” in this context, or does it more readily mean “gently/a little” without additional wording?
5. Is `راني هنا — raani hna` natural on arrival at a mutually known meeting point? What interpretations become likely outside that context?
6. Confirm whether `نتا — nta` and `نتي — nti` are suitable productive singular forms for the selected baseline and whether either is commonly reduced further in ordinary speech.
7. Can identity questions `نتا سمير؟` and `نتي نورا؟` occur naturally with intonation alone in this phone context? Compare with versions containing `واش — waash` and state pragmatic differences.
8. Does `لا. أنا لينا.` sound like a natural correction, or is a discourse marker normally expected before or after it?

### Pronunciation and rhythm

9. Record careful and ordinary versions of `ما فهمتش`. Identify the actual vowel quality and duration in `فهمت`, whether any consonant is regularly assimilated, and where the main beat falls.
10. Does ordinary `ما فهمتش` remain analysable as `ma_fhemt-sh`, or does that notation encourage an inaccurate boundary or rhythm?
11. Record `راني هنا` at careful and ordinary speed. Is there audible linking, vowel shortening or consonant adjustment at the word boundary?
12. Do central-urban speakers produce `هنا` as `hna`, `hnaaya` or another form in this exact deictic context? Keep alternatives separate by meaning and deixis.
13. How much support vowel, if any, appears before the initial clusters in `smaḥ-li`, `fhemt` and `hna`? Give acoustic or close-listening notes rather than spelling intuition alone.
14. Is the proposed main beat in `ʿawed b-shwiya` on `shwi-`, and how does polite prosody differ from an impatient command?
15. Confirm whether the transliteration `ʿawed` adequately represents the first vowel and the realisation of ع in the baseline.

### Script and Arabizi

16. Review every Arabic course form for stable pedagogical spelling without restoring unspoken MSA morphology.
17. Supply privacy-cleared, naturally occurring or elicited Arabizi spellings for `raani hna`, `ma fhemt-sh` and `ʿawed b-shwiya`. Do not generate them mechanically from course transliteration.
18. Is `mafhemtch` recognisable and plausibly Algerian in the reviewers’ digital networks? Record age band, platform and spelling alternatives; occurrence must not be converted into a universal standard.

### Regional preview

19. For the carrier word `قهوة`, obtain item-specific pronunciations from at least two Algiers/periphery reviewers and appropriately profiled reviewers from any region later represented in audio.
20. Does the wording of the `q/g/ʔ/k` box overgeneralise older or broad published descriptions? Narrow each geographical label to what current reviewers can support.
21. Do not approve a regional recording unless a second reviewer connected to the named community accepts both the form and the scope label.
