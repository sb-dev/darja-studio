# Chapter 2 — Greeting people naturally

> **Draft and validation notice:** All productive language below is proposed for a central urban baseline associated mainly with Algiers. The core forms are documented in Algerian sources, but their exact vowels, ordinary-speed reductions and pragmatic range still require two central-urban reviews. Threshold hospitality, older-person address and repetition also require `NV-PRAG` review.
>
> **Ledger notice:** The Chapter 1 ledger was not supplied with this task. The IDs below are therefore provisional and must be reconciled before publication. Pronouns or identity language already approved in Chapter 1 should be marked as recycled rather than recounted here.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- greet one person or several people;
- answer a greeting immediately;
- ask and answer “How are you?” briefly;
- show extra warmth through rhythm and limited repetition;
- welcome a guest and invite them inside;
- greet an older person respectfully without pretending that one title works everywhere;
- leave with a natural short farewell.

### Realistic situation

You have arranged to visit your friend Yasmine. Her mother opens the door. You greet her, exchange a short wellbeing check, and wait for her invitation before entering. Later, you say goodbye to the household.

---

## 2. Before you speak

You hear someone at a doorway say:

> **مرحبا بيك، تفضّلي.**  
> `marḥba bik, tfaḍḍli.`  
> “Welcome—come in.”

Without translating word by word, decide:

1. Who is probably the host?
2. Is the guest one man, one woman or a group?
3. Which part welcomes the guest, and which part moves the visit forward?

## 3. Productive vocabulary `[P]`

([asjp.cerist.dz](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/13/13/2/281442))

### Active target set

The ten new chunks above plus these four operations form the chapter’s **14-item active target set**:

1. choose `raak / raaki / raakom` by addressee;
2. choose `nta / nti / ntuma` in the return question;
3. choose `tfaḍḍal / tfaḍḍli / tfaḍḍlu` by addressee;
4. use one warm repetition without turning the exchange into a chant.

## 5. Sentence engine

### Frame C02-F01 — Open and answer

- **Function:** establish contact when arriving or entering.
- **Arabic skeleton:** `السّلام عليكم ← وعليكم السّلام`
- **Transliteration:** `s-slaam ʿlikom ← wa ʿlikom s-slaam`
- **Natural English range:** “Hello.” ← “Hello.”
- **Fixed material:** both complete formulas.
- **Replaceable slots:** none at beginner level.
- **Negation:** not applicable.
- **Pragmatic use:** answer before moving to the main business.

**Example 1**

> **السّلام عليكم.**  
> `s-slaam ʿlikom.`  
> “Hello.”
>
> **وعليكم السّلام.**  
> `wa ʿlikom s-slaam.`  
> “Hello.”

**Example 2: entering a home**

> **السّلام عليكم.**  
> `s-slaam ʿlikom.`  
> “Hello, everyone.”
>
> **وعليكم السّلام، مرحبا بيك.**  
> `wa ʿlikom s-slaam, marḥba bik.`  
> “Hello—welcome.”

**Linked frame:** C02-F03 threshold welcome.

---

### Frame C02-F02 — Check wellbeing and return the question

- **Function:** exchange a brief social wellbeing check.
- **Arabic skeleton:** `واش راك/راكي/راكم؟ ← لاباس، الحمد لله. ونتا/نتي/نتوما؟`
- **Transliteration:** `waash raak/raaki/raakom? ← la baas, l-ḥamdullah. u nta/nti/ntuma?`
- **English range:** “How are you?” ← “Fine, thank God. And you?”
- **Fixed material:** `waash`; response chunk `la baas, l-ḥamdullah`.
- **Replaceable slot:** addressee form.
- **Agreement:** see the table below.
- **Negation:** not taught in this chapter. Do not invent a negative reply by translating “not fine” word for word.
- **Pragmatic use:** often a social check rather than a request for a medical report. A close friend may nevertheless answer more fully.

| Addressee | Question | Return question |
|---|---|---|
| one man | **واش راك؟** — `waash raak?` — “How are you?” | **ونتا؟** — `u nta?` — “And you?” |
| one woman | **واش راكي؟** — `waash raaki?` — “How are you?” | **ونتي؟** — `u nti?` — “And you?” |
| group | **واش راكم؟** — `waash raakom?` — “How are you all?” | **ونتوما؟** — `u ntuma?` — “And you all?” |

**Shorter alternative**

> **لاباس؟**  
> `la baas?`  
> “All good?”

> **لاباس، الحمد لله.**  
> `la baas, l-ḥamdullah.`  
> “Fine, thank God.”

`Waash raak?` is documented as a phatic greeting question in Algerian conversational research: the expected social function can be disrupted deliberately when a respondent treats it as an intrusive literal question ([Bourahla 2013](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/23/20/3/3439), p. 129). This does not mean that every answer must be empty; relationship and context still matter. ([asjp.cerist.dz](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/23/20/3/3439))

### Frame C02-F03 — Welcome and move through the doorway

- **Function:** welcome a guest and invite them to enter.
- **Arabic skeleton:** `مرحبا بيك/بيكم. تفضّل/تفضّلي/تفضّلوا.`
- **Transliteration:** `marḥba bik/bikom. tfaḍḍal/tfaḍḍli/tfaḍḍlu.`
- **English range:** “Welcome. Come in.”
- **Fixed material:** `marḥba`; invitation stem `tfaḍḍ-`.
- **Slots:** singular/group and, for the singular invitation, gender.
- **Negation:** not used in this frame.
- **Pragmatic use:** the host says the invitation. A learner arriving as a guest should not use `tfaḍḍal` to command the host to enter.

| Guest(s) | Welcome | Invitation |
|---|---|---|
| one man | **مرحبا بيك.** — `marḥba bik.` — “Welcome.” | **تفضّل.** — `tfaḍḍal.` — “Come in.” |
| one woman | **مرحبا بيك.** — `marḥba bik.` — “Welcome.” | **تفضّلي.** — `tfaḍḍli.` — “Come in.” |
| group | **مرحبا بيكم.** — `marḥba bikom.` — “Welcome, everyone.” | **تفضّلوا.** — `tfaḍḍlu.` — “Come in.” |

**Warm repetition**

> **مرحبا بيك، مرحبا.**  
> `marḥba bik, marḥba.`  
> “You’re very welcome.”

> **تفضّلي، تفضّلي.**  
> `tfaḍḍli, tfaḍḍli.`  
> “Come in, come in.”

The English translations show the social effect rather than counting each repeated word separately. The amount, timing and pitch of repetition require native pragmatic and audio review.

## 6. Accessible dialogue

### At Yasmine’s family home

**Purpose:** Lina has arrived for a planned visit. Nadia, Yasmine’s mother, establishes who is at the door socially—not through an identity interrogation—and invites her inside.

**Speakers:**

- **Lina:** young adult guest, female.
- **Nadia:** older adult host, female; mother of Lina’s friend.

1. **Lina:** **السّلام عليكم.**  
   `s-slaam ʿlikom.`  
   “Hello.”

2. **Nadia:** **وعليكم السّلام. مرحبا بيك، لاباس؟**  
   `wa ʿlikom s-slaam. marḥba bik, la baas?`  
   “Hello. Welcome—are you well?”

3. **Lina:** **لاباس، الحمد لله. ونتي؟**  
   `la baas, l-ḥamdullah. u nti?`  
   “Fine, thank God. And you?”

4. **Nadia:** **الحمد لله. تفضّلي، تفضّلي.**  
   `l-ḥamdullah. tfaḍḍli, tfaḍḍli.`  
   “Thank God. Come in, come in.”

5. **Lina:** **الحمد لله.**  
   `l-ḥamdullah.`  
   “Thank you / Gladly.”

> **Validation flag on line 5:** `l-ḥamdullah` as an accepting response at this exact threshold moment is plausible but not yet approved. Reviewers should compare it with silence plus a smile, `yaʿṭik ṣ-ṣaḥḥa`, `Allah ysellemek`, or another locally preferred response. If reviewers reject it, remove the line rather than teaching an artificial reply.

### Leaving later

6. **Lina:** **بالسلامة.**  
   `b-slaama.`  
   “Goodbye.”

7. **Nadia:** **بالسلامة.**  
   `b-slaama.`  
   “Take care.”

> **Validation flag:** Confirm reciprocal `b-slaama` in this relationship and whether the host more naturally adds another departure wish. Only `b-slaama` is productive in this draft.

## 7. Understand the exchange

Answer without translating every word.

1. Who arrives: Lina or Nadia?
2. Which line makes Lina explicitly welcome?
3. How do you know Nadia is speaking to one woman?
4. Does Lina give a detailed account of her health?
5. What non-linguistic result occurs after line 4?
6. Which repeated expression sounds like an invitation rather than hesitation?

**Answers:** 1. Lina. 2. Line 2. 3. `tfaḍḍli` in line 4; `nti` also refers to a woman. 4. No. 5. Lina is invited to enter. 6. `tfaḍḍli, tfaḍḍli`.

## 8. Ordinary-speed dialogue

The ordinary version is shorter. The speakers know why Lina is visiting, so they do not explain it at the doorway.

### Continuous version

**Lina:** **سلام عليكم.**  
`slaam ʿlikom.`  
“Hello.”

**Nadia:** **وعليكم السّلام، مرحبا بيك. لاباس؟**  
`wa ʿlikom s-slaam, marḥba bik. la baas?`  
“Hello—welcome. All good?”

**Lina:** **لاباس الحمد لله، ونتي؟**  
`la baas l-ḥamdullah, u nti?`  
“Fine, thank God. And you?”

**Nadia:** **الحمد لله. تفضّلي تفضّلي.**  
`l-ḥamdullah. tfaḍḍli tfaḍḍli.`  
“Thank God. Come in, come in.”

### Connected-speech guide

`slaam_ʿlikom // wa_ʿlikom_s-slaam / marḥba_bik / la_baas? // la_baas_l-ḥamdullah / u_nti? // l-ḥamdullah / tfaḍḍli_tfaḍḍli //`

The guide marks rhythm groups, not mandatory pauses or a narrow phonetic transcription. Final reductions must follow the approved human recording.

## 10. Pronunciation and rhythm

### Primary target: keep common phrases in one rhythm group

Do not give every written syllable equal weight. These are social chunks:

| Careful phrase | Natural grouping | Main beat | Learner warning |
|---|---|---|---|
| `s-slaam ʿlikom` | `s-slaam_ʿlikom` | **slaam** | Do not say five separated English-like words. |
| `wa ʿlikom s-slaam` | `wa_ʿlikom_s-slaam` | **slaam** | The opening `wa` is short. |
| `waash raak?` | `waash_raak?` | **raak** | Let the question rise or remain open according to the model; do not add English “do.” |
| `marḥba bik` | `marḥba_bik` | **marḥ** or **bik**, depending on warm focus | Keep `ḥ`, written ح, stronger than English `h`. |
| `b-slaama` | `b-slaama` | **slaa** | Do not insert full vowels and produce `bə-sə-laa-ma`. |

### The sound `ḥ`

In **مرحبا** `marḥba` and **الحمد لله** `l-ḥamdullah`, `ḥ` represents ح: a stronger breathy/pharyngeal sound than English `h`. At first, aim for clear airflow without squeezing painfully.

### The doubled `ḍḍ`

In **تفضّل** `tfaḍḍal`, hold the middle consonant slightly:

- not: `tfa-ḍal` with a quick single consonant;
- target: `tfaḍ-ḍal` with a perceptible hold.

The emphatic quality and the amount of gemination must follow human audio. Do not turn the word into syllable-by-syllable theatre.

### Shadowing sequence

1. **Careful:** `marḥba bik. tfaḍḍli.`
2. **Natural:** `marḥba_bik / tfaḍḍli.`
3. **Warm repetition:** `marḥba_bik / tfaḍḍli_tfaḍḍli.`
4. **Response contrast:** hear a host address a man, a woman and a group; point to the matching picture before repeating.

## 11. Pragmatics and social guidance

### 11.1 Answer the greeting first

When someone opens with **السّلام عليكم**, use **وعليكم السّلام** before moving to the visit’s purpose. Greeting sequences are interactional openings, not decorative vocabulary. Algerian research on conversational openings treats greetings as socially meaningful sequences and positive-politeness resources rather than isolated words ([Revue Algérienne des Sciences Humaines et Sociales 2024](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/259/12/2/259305), pp. 186–196). ([asjp.cerist.dz](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/259/12/2/259305))

### 11.2 Warmth through limited repetition

A host may repeat **مرحبا** or **تفضّلي** to reinforce welcome. Do not assume that more repetition is always warmer. Relationship, pitch, speed, gesture and whether the host is busy all matter. The anchor dialogue therefore requires human recording and `NV-PRAG` approval.

### 11.3 Older people: respect is not one magic title

A clear greeting, an immediate response, patient pacing and an appropriate wellbeing question are safer beginner strategies than guessing a title. A study based in the extreme west of Algeria documents varied respectful choices—including kin terms, titles, French forms and avoidance—and explicitly warns that preferences differ among speaker groups ([Boumedini 2021](https://aleph.edinum.org/3718?lang=en), §§4.2.2–5). ([aleph.edinum.org](https://aleph.edinum.org/3718?lang=en))

Do **not** assume:

- that every older man should be called `l-ḥaajj`;
- that every older woman should be called `l-ḥaajja` or `khaalti`;
- that a plural “you” automatically creates respectful singular address;
- that first-name use has the same force in every family.

The western study reports that most of its informants did not treat the independent plural pronoun as a normal everyday honorific singular, though plural verb marking and titles require more contextual study. This finding must not be projected automatically onto Algiers or all Algeria. ([aleph.edinum.org](https://aleph.edinum.org/3718?lang=en))

### 11.4 Entering a home

At the threshold:

1. greet the people who are present;
2. allow the host to make the invitation clear;
3. follow household cues rather than assuming one national rule about physical greetings, footwear or where to sit;
4. use the group form if the host is welcoming several guests together.

### 11.5 Physical greetings

This chapter teaches verbal language only. Handshakes, cheek-kissing and other contact depend on relationship, gender, family preference, religious practice and personal comfort. A smile, nod and spoken greeting provide a safe non-contact route. Do not force physical contact to appear “authentic.”

### 11.6 French greetings

You may hear `bonjour` or `bonsoir` inside an otherwise Darja interaction. Treat this as active French code-switching, not as proof that French is required or preferred by every Algerian. Algerian Arabic–French switching is well documented, but its density and function vary across speakers and settings ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf)). ([bucket.theses-algerie.com](https://bucket.theses-algerie.com/files/repositories-dz/2482449201796942.pdf))

- **You may hear:** **عمي** `ʿammi`, **خالتي** `khaalti`, **الحاج** `l-ḥaajj`, **الحاجة** `l-ḥaajja`.

### 3. French greeting switches

- **Course baseline:** **السّلام عليكم** — `s-slaam ʿlikom`.
- **You may hear:** `bonjour`, `bonsoir` or `salut`.
- **Difference:** active French code-switching, not an integrated Darja inflection.
- **Learner action:** recognise. Deliberate productive switching is deferred.
- **Evidence limit:** online Algerian data establish occurrence, not national frequency or a fixed social meaning. `NV-CS-OPEN`. ([bucket.theses-algerie.com](https://bucket.theses-algerie.com/files/repositories-dz/2482449201796942.pdf))

## 13. Rooted context

### Greeting as relationship work

**Claim type: `EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`, based on documented conversational evidence.**

The wellbeing question in this chapter does more than request information. It acknowledges the other person and creates a bridge into the visit. Research using Algerian examples shows that **واش راك؟** can serve a phatic or contact-maintaining function; deliberately hostile or over-literal replies can terminate that contact. ([asjp.cerist.dz](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/downArticle/23/20/3/3439))

The course therefore teaches the whole sequence:

> greeting → response → wellbeing check → returned question → welcome/invitation

It does not teach **واش راك؟** as an isolated phrase to fire at strangers.

### No decorative colonial-history insert

Colonial history is not added to this chapter merely because a French greeting may occur. Contemporary French switches are taught here through documented multilingual practice. A later chapter can examine language hierarchy or historical policy only when the communicative content makes that mechanism relevant and the evidence separates policy, present practice and speaker motivation.

6. **تفضّلوا.** — `tfaḍḍlu.` — “Come in.”

### B. Choose

Choose the most suitable response.

1. Someone says **السّلام عليكم**.
   - a. **وعليكم السّلام**
   - b. **تفضّلوا**

2. A female host asks **واش راكي؟**
   - a. **لاباس، الحمد لله. ونتي؟**
   - b. **مرحبا بيكم**

3. Two guests are at your door.
   - a. **مرحبا بيك. تفضّلي.**
   - b. **مرحبا بيكم. تفضّلوا.**

**Answers:** 1a, 2a, 3b.

### C. Substitution

Change only the addressee. Preserve the purpose.

**Model: one man**

> **واش راك؟**  
> `waash raak?`  
> “How are you?”

1. one woman → **واش راكي؟** — `waash raaki?`
2. a group → **واش راكم؟** — `waash raakom?`

**Model: invite one man inside**

> **مرحبا بيك. تفضّل.**  
> `marḥba bik. tfaḍḍal.`

1. one woman → **مرحبا بيك. تفضّلي.** — `marḥba bik. tfaḍḍli.`
2. a group → **مرحبا بيكم. تفضّلوا.** — `marḥba bikom. tfaḍḍlu.`

### D. Cued retrieval

Cover the Darja. Respond aloud within four seconds.

1. “Hello” on entering → **السّلام عليكم.** — `s-slaam ʿlikom.`
2. Reply to that greeting → **وعليكم السّلام.** — `wa ʿlikom s-slaam.`
3. “How are you?” to one woman → **واش راكي؟** — `waash raaki?`
4. “Fine, thank God. And you?” to one man → **لاباس، الحمد لله. ونتا؟** — `la baas, l-ḥamdullah. u nta?`
5. “Welcome” to a group → **مرحبا بيكم.** — `marḥba bikom.`
6. “Come in” to one woman → **تفضّلي.** — `tfaḍḍli.`
7. “Goodbye” → **بالسلامة.** — `b-slaama.`

### E. Timed response

Play or read each cue. Begin within two seconds.

### G. Repair round

## 15. Listening work

### Listening A — Who is at the door?

The learner hears three threshold welcomes in random order:

1. **مرحبا بيك. تفضّل.**  
   `marḥba bik. tfaḍḍal.`  
   “Welcome. Come in.”

2. **مرحبا بيك. تفضّلي.**  
   `marḥba bik. tfaḍḍli.`  
   “Welcome. Come in.”

3. **مرحبا بيكم. تفضّلوا.**  
   `marḥba bikom. tfaḍḍlu.`  
   “Welcome, everyone. Come in.”

**First listen — purpose:** choose the picture showing one man, one woman or a group.

**Second listen — key detail:** identify the ending: `-al`, `-li` or `-lu`.

**Third listen — wording and rhythm:** shadow only the final invitation.

**Transcript check:** compare the three Arabic and transliteration lines.

**Transfer question:** Which form would your household need most often when several friends arrive together?

### Listening B — Warm or merely repeated?

Hear two human recordings:

- **تفضّلي.** — `tfaḍḍli.` — “Come in.”
- **تفضّلي، تفضّلي.** — `tfaḍḍli, tfaḍḍli.` — “Come in, come in.”

Choose which sounds warmer, then identify the audio evidence: pitch, timing, volume and gesture implied by the voice. The answer must be based on the approved recording, not on repetition alone.

### Listening C — Recognition only

Hear one Darja opening and one French-switched opening. Mark **D** or **F**:

- **السّلام عليكم.** — `s-slaam ʿlikom.` — “Hello.”
- `Bonjour.` — “Hello.”

Do not imitate the French-switch clip until its setting and speaker profile have been discussed.

## 16. Information-gap speaking task

### The apartment-door mix-up

**Non-linguistic outcome:** identify which visitor or group has arrived and direct the correct person or people inside.

#### Role A — Host

You have three expected arrivals, but your card shows only the welcome you must use:

1. `marḥba bik — tfaḍḍal`
2. `marḥba bik — tfaḍḍli`
3. `marḥba bikom — tfaḍḍlu`

Do not show the card. Open with a greeting, exchange a wellbeing check, then welcome the visitor correctly.

#### Role B — Visitor

Your card shows one identity only:

- one man;
- one woman;
- a two-person group.

Do not announce the grammar label. Reply to the greeting and wellbeing check. If the host chooses the wrong invitation, signal the mismatch and require a repeated welcome.

#### Required language

- **السّلام عليكم** — `s-slaam ʿlikom` — “Hello.”
- **وعليكم السّلام** — `wa ʿlikom s-slaam` — reply.
- one `waash raak…?` form;
- one short wellbeing answer;
- the correct welcome and invitation.

#### Required repair event

In round 2, the host must deliberately choose one wrong singular/group form. The visitor may not enter until the host repeats the corrected line.

#### Self-study adaptation

1. Make three concealed cards: MAN, WOMAN, GROUP.
2. Shuffle and reveal one card after saying the opening greeting.
3. Produce the complete exchange without looking at the chapter.
4. Record yourself.
5. Check only the addressee endings first; on a second replay, check rhythm and response timing.

## 19. Audio pack and recording directions

### Shared recording rules

- **Baseline speakers:** people linguistically socialised mainly in Algiers or its close urban periphery; record self-described profile rather than assigning an accent from appearance.
- **Anchor-dialogue relationship:** younger adult guest and older adult host who know each other through a family relationship.
- **Setting:** apartment or house doorway during a planned daytime visit.
- **No fabricated accent:** generic TTS may prototype careful drills only.
- **Human required:** all dialogue masters, ordinary-speed lines, warm repetition, older-person pragmatics and French-switch recognition.
- **Code-switching:** none in the core dialogue. The `bonjour` recognition clip is a deliberate lexical French switch and requires a bilingual human speaker.
- **Main pronunciation risks:** `ʿ`, `ḥ`, doubled `ḍḍ`, consonant clusters, `b-slaama`, plural endings, and over-articulating Arabic-script vowels.
- **Validation:** all records remain `NV-OPEN` until script, pragmatics and audio review.

- Record each dialogue as a full interaction before extracting or rerecording lines.
