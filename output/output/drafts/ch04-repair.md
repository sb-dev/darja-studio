# Chapter 4 — Understanding and repairing conversation

> **Editorial status:** Draft for native review. All new productive forms remain `NV-OPEN`, even where documentary or informal occurrence evidence exists. In particular, reviewers must confirm central-Algiers pronunciation, politeness, Arabic spelling and ordinary-speed reductions before publication.

## 1. Outcome and situation

By the end of this chapter, you can:

- say that you did not understand;
- ask someone to repeat or speak more slowly;
- ask what a word means;
- ask how to say something in Darja;
- correct a mistaken name or idea without turning the exchange into a confrontation;
- recognise when a speaker briefly switches to French to clarify or accommodate.

**Situation:** You are signing in at a neighbourhood language exchange. The organiser mishears your name and later switches briefly into French. You keep the conversation moving instead of pretending to understand.

Repair is not failure. Studies of Algerian bilingual interaction document repetition, questioning, reformulation and code-switching among the resources speakers use when communication breaks down, although the available studies do not justify treating one strategy as universal across Algeria ([Belgrimet 2020](https://ijels.com/detail/a-conversation-analysis-of-repair-strategies-in-communication-breakdowns-a-case-study-of-algerian-bilingual-students/), pp. 459–467). ([ijels.com](https://ijels.com/detail/a-conversation-analysis-of-repair-strategies-in-communication-breakdowns-a-case-study-of-algerian-bilingual-students/))

## 2. Before you speak

You hear a sentence but understand only one word. What is usually more useful?

1. smiling and pretending;
2. saying only “What?”;
3. showing the problem and requesting a specific repair.

This chapter builds option 3:

> **signal the problem → request help → listen again → confirm or correct**

### Meaning limits

- `ma fhemt-sh` can cover “I didn’t understand” or “I didn’t catch that.” It does not blame the other speaker.
- `b-shwiya` is taught **inside a repetition request**, not as a universal translation of English *slowly*.
- `yaʿni` can explain another person’s wording or reformulate your own wording.
- `qṣdi` introduces what **you intended**. Do not mechanically use it every time English uses *I mean*.
- `maashi` corrects an identification or description. Full verbal negation is taught later.

## 5. Sentence engine

### Frame C04-F01 — Signal non-understanding and request a repair

- **Function:** stop a breakdown without ending the conversation.
- **Arabic skeleton:** `سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود [بشوية]، [من فضلك].`
- **Transliteration:** `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed [b-shwiya], [men faḍlek].`
- **Natural range:** “Sorry, I didn’t understand. Say it again [more slowly], [please].”
- **Fixed material:** `ma fhemt-sh`; imperative `ʿaawed`.
- **Optional slots:** `smaḥli`, `b-shwiya`, `men faḍlek`.
- **Constraint:** use `b-shwiya` to request reduced speed, not to accuse the speaker of speaking badly.
- **Negation:** `ma …-sh` is contained in the memorised past-form chunk `ma fhemt-sh`.
- **Pragmatic use:** with a stranger, include `smaḥli`, `men faḍlek`, warm intonation, or a combination. You do not need all softeners in every turn.

**Examples**

1. سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود، من فضلك.  
   `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed, men faḍlek.`  
   Sorry, I didn’t understand. Repeat, please.

2. ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية.  
   `ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`  
   I didn’t get that. Say it again more slowly.

**Links:** earlier apology/politeness frames → later extended repair and paraphrase frames.

### Frame C04-F02 — Ask for meaning or wording

#### Meaning

- **Arabic skeleton:** `[هذي الكلمة] واش معناها؟`
- **Transliteration:** `[haadi l-kelma] wash maʿna-ha?`
- **English:** “What does [this word] mean?”

#### Wording

- **Arabic skeleton:** `كيفاش نقولو “[X]” بالدارجة؟`
- **Transliteration:** `kiifash ngulu “[X]” b-ddarža?`
- **English:** “How do we say ‘[X]’ in Darja?”
- **Slot constraint:** `[X]` may be an English word, a French word, a gesture or an idea both speakers can identify.
- **Code-switching:** the quoted unknown word stays in its original language or script.

**Examples**

1. هذي الكلمة واش معناها؟  
   `haadi l-kelma wash maʿna-ha?`  
   What does this word mean?

2. كيفاش نقولو “slowly” بالدارجة؟  
   `kiifash ngulu “slowly” b-ddarža?`  
   How do we say “slowly” in Darja?

### Frame C04-F03 — Correct and reformulate politely

- **Arabic skeleton:** `لا، ماشي [X]. قصدي [Y].`
- **Transliteration:** `laa, maashi [X]. qṣdi [Y].`
- **English:** “No, not [X]. I mean [Y].”
- **Slots:** `[X]` is the mistaken item; `[Y]` is the correction.
- **Constraint:** keep `[X]` short. Repeating a person’s entire mistaken sentence may sound argumentative.
- **Alternative explanation:** `[X]، يعني [Y].` — “[X], meaning [Y].”

**Examples**

1. لا، ماشي سامي. قصدي سام.  
   `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`  
   No, not Sami. I mean Sam.

2. بشوية، يعني ما تزربش.  
   `b-shwiya, yaʿni ma tezreb-sh.`  
   More slowly—that is, don’t rush.  
   **Listening example only:** `ma tezreb-sh` is not yet productive.

## 6. Accessible dialogue

### Need

A volunteer is entering your name. They hear **Sami**, but your name is **Sam**. The practical result is a corrected sign-in entry.

### Dialogue

**Organiser:** اسمك سامي؟  
`smek Saami?`  
Is your name Sami?

**Visitor:** سمحلي، ما فهمتش. عاود بشوية، من فضلك.  
`smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya, men faḍlek.`  
Sorry, I didn’t understand. Say it again more slowly, please.

**Organiser:** اسمك سامي؟  
`smek Saami?`  
Is your name Sami?

**Visitor:** لا، ماشي سامي. قصدي سام.  
`laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`  
No, not Sami. I mean Sam.

**Organiser:** آه، سام. فهمت.  
`aah, Sam. fhemt.`  
Oh, Sam. Got it.

### Understand the exchange

1. What practical task is the organiser completing?
2. Did the visitor immediately know what the organiser had asked?
3. Which phrase requested both repetition and lower speed?
4. What was the mistaken name?
5. What was the final corrected name?

**Answers:** 1. recording the visitor’s name; 2. no; 3. `ʿaawed b-shwiya`; 4. Sami; 5. Sam.

## 7. Ordinary-speed dialogue

### Need

After correcting the name, the organiser checks which language will work. They try French; the visitor requests an explanation and then states a preference for Darja.

### Performance note

The French turn is a deliberate accommodation attempt, not proof that every Algerian speaker would switch in this situation. The visitor manages the switch rather than pretending to understand it.

### Dialogue master

**Organiser:** اسمك سامي؟  
`smek Saami?`  
Your name’s Sami?

**Visitor:** سمحلي؟  
`smaḥli?`  
Sorry?

**Organiser:** سامي، صح؟  
`Saami, ṣaḥḥ?`  
Sami, right?

**Visitor:** لا لا، سام. قصدي سام.  
`laa laa, Sam. qṣdi Sam.`  
No, no—Sam. I mean Sam.

**Organiser:** آه، سام. Tu parles français ?  
`aah, Sam. Tu parles français ?`  
Oh, Sam. Do you speak French?

**Visitor:** سمحلي، واش معناها؟  
`smaḥli, wash maʿna-ha?`  
Sorry, what does that mean?

**Organiser:** يعني: تهدر بالفرنسية؟  
`yaʿni: tehder b-l-fransawiyya?`  
It means: do you speak French?

**Visitor:** آه، فهمت. شوية، بصح بالدارجة من فضلك.  
`aah, fhemt. shwiya, bṣaḥ b-ddarža men faḍlek.`  
Oh, I understand. A little, but in Darja, please.

**Organiser:** صحا.  
`ṣaḥḥa.`  
Okay/thanks.

### Controlled-density notes

- `ṣaḥḥ?`, `tehder b-l-fransawiyya?`, `bṣaḥ` and turn-final `ṣaḥḥa` are recycled or listening-support material, not new Chapter 4 productive items.
- The French sentence is an active switch, tagged `FR-CS`; it is not an integrated Darja loan.
- A human bilingual recording is required because the transition into and out of French is part of the lesson.

## 8. Pronunciation and rhythm

### Primary target: keep the negative frame together

Do not pronounce `ma` and `-sh` as two unrelated words. They frame the verb:

- careful: `ma fhemt-sh`
- rhythm group: `ma_fhemt-sh`
- natural response: `smaḥli / ma_fhemt-sh //`

The main beat falls on **fhemt**, not on a long English-like vowel in `ma`:

> `smaḥli / ma_**FHEMT**-sh // ʿaawed_b-**SHWI**ya //`

### Consonant cluster

`fhemt` begins with `fh-`. Avoid inserting a strong vowel such as **fa-hemt** unless the reviewed recording has a brief central transition. Start with `f`, release directly into `h`, then continue to `emt`.

### Long vowel and pharyngeal onset

- `ʿaawed` begins with `ʿ`. A beginner approximation is acceptable initially, but do not replace it in writing.
- Hold `aa` slightly: `ʿaa-wed`.
- Do not turn `ʿaawed` into English **owed**.

### Correction rhythm

Use two short groups, with the correction carrying the strongest beat:

> `laa / maashi **SAA**mi // qṣdi **SAM** //`

The pitch should settle on the corrected item. A sharp rise plus high volume can make the correction sound confrontational.

### Shadowing groups

1. `smaḥli / ma_fhemt-sh //`
2. `ʿaawed_b-shwiya / men_faḍlek //`
3. `laa / maashi Saami // qṣdi Sam //`
4. `smaḥli / wash_maʿna-ha? //`

## 9. Pragmatics

### Be specific about the problem

`ma fhemt-sh` alone is useful. Adding a specific request is better:

- repeat: `ʿaawed`;
- reduce speed: `ʿaawed b-shwiya`;
- explain a word: `wash maʿna-ha?`;
- give the Darja wording: `kiifash ngulu… b-ddarža?`.

This tells the other person how to help.

### Directness and warmth

A bare imperative such as `ʿaawed` can be efficient among supportive peers. With a stranger, employee, elder or person who is already making an effort, add a softener and use a cooperative tone:

- `smaḥli, ʿaawed.`
- `ʿaawed, men faḍlek.`
- `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh.`

Do not stack every polite expression mechanically. Prosody, relationship and facial expression also affect how the request is heard. The exact social distribution requires `NV-PRAG` review.

### Correct the message, not the person

Prefer:

> `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`

Avoid an accusatory English-calqued message such as “You said my name wrong.” The short Darja correction resolves the task without assigning blame.

### Translation is not surrender

If both people share another language, a brief translation can be efficient. If they do not, ask for repetition, a simpler explanation, a gesture or an example. Do not assume that French will help every Algerian interlocutor—or every learner.

## 10. You may hear…

### 1. An alternative non-understanding frame

**Baseline productive form:** `ma fhemt-sh` — I didn’t understand.

**You may hear:** `ma raanish fahem` from a man or `ma raanish faahma` from a woman — roughly, “I’m not following.”

- **Difference:** grammatical framing and gender agreement.
- **Learner action:** recognise only for now.
- **Scope:** reported in Algerian learning and reference material, but central frequency and pragmatic contrast require review.
- **Status:** `NV-OPEN`.

### 2. A French repair turn

Among some bilingual speakers and in some settings, you may hear a brief French **Pardon ?** or another French clarification turn.

- **Difference:** active code-switch, not ordinary Darja vocabulary.
- **Learner action:** recognise it; do not treat it as compulsory or socially neutral everywhere.
- **Scope:** Algerian Arabic–French switching is documented in spoken and online corpora, but density and function vary by speaker and situation ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf), pp. 1–2). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/))
- **Status:** exact repair token `NV-CS`.

### 3. Words built on `ʿqel`

In some communities and phrases, forms such as `bel-ʿqel` or `b-ʿaqlek` may ask someone to calm down, take care, lower their voice or go slowly.

- **Difference:** vocabulary and pragmatics, not simply pronunciation.
- **Learner action:** recognise contextually; continue producing `ʿaawed b-shwiya` for this chapter’s slower-repetition request.
- **Evidence limit:** the cited description is local to Fadj M’Zala/Ferdjioua and does not establish use throughout eastern Algeria or Algeria as a whole. ([opac.univ-mila.dz](https://opac.univ-mila.dz/z/800-3879.pdf))
- **Status:** `NV-REGION` required before any local production recommendation.

## 11. Rooted context — Using the whole repertoire

**Claim classification:** `DOC-POLICY`, `EDITORIAL-INTERPRETATION`.

Algerian language practice takes place within a multilingual landscape involving Darja, multiple Tamazight varieties, formal Arabic, French and—depending on speaker and setting—other languages. Scholarship links present language practices to education, access, identity, colonial and postcolonial policy, and globalisation; it does not support reducing every individual switch to one motive ([Rouabah 2022](https://www.thebritishacademy.ac.uk/publishing/journal-british-academy/10s4/multilingualism-in-algeria/), pp. 21–40). ([thebritishacademy.ac.uk](https://www.thebritishacademy.ac.uk/publishing/journal-british-academy/10s4/multilingualism-in-algeria/))

French has a documented historical and institutional relationship to colonisation and later Algerian language policy. Contemporary Arabic–French switching, however, may serve many immediate purposes: naming a familiar concept, handling a technical domain, quoting someone, accommodating an interlocutor, reformulating or repairing a breakdown. Corpora establish that switching occurs; they do not prove that every speaker switches in the same way or for the same reason ([Amazouz, Adda-Decker & Lamel 2018](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/); [Cotterell et al. 2014](https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~ccb/publications/arabic-french-codeswitching.pdf)). ([aclanthology.org](https://aclanthology.org/L18-1233/))

**Course interpretation:** choosing the resource that restores shared understanding is practical interactional intelligence. That may mean staying in Darja, requesting a simpler Darja explanation, translating briefly, switching languages, pointing or giving an example. Multilingual practice should not automatically be labelled linguistic deficiency, but neither should learners assume that French is necessary for authentic Algerian speech ([Bouchiba Ghlamallah 2015](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/article/6350)). ([asjp.cerist.dz](https://asjp.cerist.dz/en/article/6350?utm_source=openai))

## 12. Practice ladder

### A. Notice

Underline the part that signals the problem; circle the part that requests a solution.

1. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed.`
2. `ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
3. `smaḥli, wash maʿna-ha?`

### B. Choose the useful response

1. You heard the sentence but it was too fast.  
   a. `qṣdi Sam.`  
   b. `ʿaawed b-shwiya.`  
   c. `kiifash ngulu Sam?`

2. You understand the sentence except for one word.  
   a. `haadi l-kelma wash maʿna-ha?`  
   b. `maashi.`  
   c. `laa laa.`

3. Someone calls you **Sami**, but your name is **Sam**.  
   a. `yaʿni Saami.`  
   b. `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`  
   c. `ʿaawed Saami.`

**Answers:** 1b, 2a, 3b.

### C. Substitution

Keep the communicative purpose stable.

#### Request repair

Model: `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed.`

Add:

1. “please”;
2. “more slowly”;
3. both “more slowly” and “please.”

Possible responses:

1. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed, men faḍlek.`
2. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya.`
3. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh. ʿaawed b-shwiya, men faḍlek.`

#### Correct an item

Model: `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`

Replace the names:

- Lina → Lena
- Adam → Ed
- Sara → Sarah, using the pronunciation intended by the learner

The names are task slots, not Darja vocabulary.

### D. Cued retrieval

Cover the Darja and produce it from the meaning.

1. Sorry, I didn’t understand.
2. Repeat more slowly, please.
3. What does this word mean?
4. How do we say “repeat” in Darja?
5. No, not Sami. I mean Sam.
6. It means…

**Key**

1. `smaḥli, ma fhemt-sh.`
2. `ʿaawed b-shwiya, men faḍlek.`
3. `haadi l-kelma wash maʿna-ha?`
4. `kiifash ngulu “repeat” b-ddarža?`
5. `laa, maashi Saami. qṣdi Sam.`
6. `yaʿni…`

### E. Timed response

Give yourself three seconds after each cue.

| Interlocutor cue | Your response goal |
|---|---|
| A fast, unclear sentence | signal non-understanding + request slower repetition |
| An unknown word | ask its meaning |
| “Your name is Sami?” | correct it to Sam |
| “How do you say *please*?” | ask for the Darja wording if you do not know |
| A French sentence you do not understand | ask what it means without criticising the switch |

### F. Mixed retrieval with earlier material

Use earlier greetings, identity and language material where available.

1. Greet the organiser.
2. Give your name.
3. Fail to understand one question.
4. Request repetition.
5. Correct one mistaken detail.
6. State which language you want the organiser to use.
7. Close the exchange using an earlier closing.

### G. Repair round

Repeat any previous activity, but your partner must create one natural breakdown:

- speak one known line too quickly;
- mishear one name;
- use one labelled French switch;
- point to one word and ask what it means.

The learner must repair the breakdown and then finish the original task. A repair that does not return to the task is incomplete.

## 20. Native-review questions and flags

### Productive form review

1. For a broad contemporary Algiers/periphery baseline, is `ma fhemt-sh` the preferred beginner form for “I didn’t understand/I didn’t catch that”? In which contexts would reviewers prefer `ma raanish fahem/faahma`?
2. Is the imperative best represented and pronounced here as `ʿaawed`, `ʿawed`, or another vowel pattern? Does the preferred form vary with the object being repeated?
3. Is `ʿaawed b-shwiya` natural as “repeat more slowly,” or would central reviewers prefer another phrase such as a request built with `tehder`? **Do not publish productively until resolved.**
4. Is `men faḍlek` ordinary and warm in this exact stranger interaction, possible but formal, or less natural than another softener?
5. Is `wash maʿna-ha?` sufficient when referring to a French sentence, or is a demonstrative or explicit noun normally preferred?
6. Confirm the preferred course Arabic spelling and pronunciation of `qṣdi`. Is a `q`, `g` or another reflex natural for this lexical item in the represented baseline?
7. Is `kiifash ngulu “[X]” b-ddarža?` natural when an English-speaking learner asks an Algerian conversation partner, or would singular/plural/addressee framing usually differ?
8. Is `maashi X, qṣdi Y` a polite neutral correction with the proposed intonation?
9. Does `yaʿni` naturally introduce the Darja translation in Dialogue 2?

### Dialogue and pragmatics review

10. Does a volunteer asking `smek Saami?` sound plausible at a sign-in table, or should the earlier-course identity frame be substituted?
11. Is bare `smaḥli?` a natural repair initiation here? Does its intonation differ clearly from an apology?
12. Is `Saami, ṣaḥḥ?` appropriate for the represented central baseline and relationship?
13. Would `Tu parles français ?` be a plausible accommodation attempt from this speaker profile without additional lead-in? Mark `NV-CS`.
14. Is `tehder b-l-fransawiyya?` natural as the immediate Darja explanation, or is another noun/adjective preferred?
15. Is the visitor’s response `shwiya, bṣaḥ b-ddarža men faḍlek` natural, or should word order or the connector change?
16. Is turn-final `ṣaḥḥa` a suitable acceptance/closure here? If not, replace it with already-taught material rather than adding an unnecessary new item.
17. Does repetition of `laa laa` sound friendly with the proposed prosody, or too forceful between strangers?

### Regional and recognition review

18. Confirm whether `bel-ʿqel/b-ʿaqlek` should remain a local eastern recognition note, be broadened cautiously, or be removed because the cited local semantic description does not match the intended repair context closely enough.
19. Elicit naturally occurring alternatives for “I didn’t hear properly” and compare them with `ma smeʿt-sh mliiḥ`.
20. Obtain at least one additional region-connected review before attaching any geographical label to a French repair token such as `Pardon ?`.

### Script and audio review
